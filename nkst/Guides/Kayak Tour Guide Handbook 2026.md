*New Orleans Kayak Swamp Tours — Gravity Trails LLC*

> Guides are responsible for creating a safe, organized, and memorable experience for every guest while representing the company with professionalism and care.

## Guide Standards

- Prioritize guest safety at all times
- Provide a welcoming and professional experience
- Communicate clearly with guests before and during the tour
- Care for all equipment and company vehicles
- Leave the bayou cleaner and better than we found it
- Enjoy connecting people with some of the most beautiful nature they will ever see

---

## Tour Guide Responsibilities

### Night Before the Tour
- Contact guests via text
- Confirm pickup location and pickup time
- Ensure guests clearly understand where to meet
- Provide your phone number in case guests need assistance
- Answer any last-minute questions

### Van Safety Check
- Inspect tires and exterior of vehicle for safety concerns
- Walk around the vehicle before driving
- Check the inside of the van for trash
- Ensure the van is clean and ready for guests
- Confirm equipment (PFDs & add-ons) needed for the tour is loaded

### Guest Arrival
- Greet guests and welcome them to the tour
- Confirm reservations and make sure everyone signed the waivers
- Explain drive & stop for bathroom en route

### On-Water Safety Orientation
- Fit guests with life jackets — **required for all guests at all times on the water**
- Explain the tour route and what guests can expect
- Demonstrate paddling technique and steering
- Review safety rules and capsize procedures

### On the Water
- Keep the group together and maintain safe spacing
- Monitor guests for fatigue or difficulty paddling
- Assist guests if needed
- Maintain awareness of surroundings and boat traffic
- Adjust pace to match the group
- Share information about wildlife, ecology, and the bayou environment

### End of Tour
- Help guests safely exit kayaks
- Collect paddles and life jackets
- Load kayaks back onto racks / trailers (facing same direction), secure straps, hitch trailer correctly
- Thank guests, ask for a review and tip
- Return trailer if end of day — secure the lot, ready for next tour
- Return guests to drop-off location

### Post-Tour Responsibilities
- Clean any trash from the van
- Ensure the van has sufficient gas for the next tour
- Enter gas mileage correctly if fueling
- Report any vehicle or equipment issues to management
- Hang life jackets to dry properly
- Ensure the Piety Street base location is secure
- Lock car, container, and gate before leaving

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Forgetting to contact guests the night before
2. Not checking van tires or fuel level
3. Skipping the safety briefing
4. Launching before all waivers are signed
5. Leaving equipment wet or improperly stored
6. Forgetting to lock the Piety Street base location

## Manchac Training — Bayou Wildlife & Ecology Talking Points

*These are optional facts to share during tours. Build your own tour personality around this framework. Aim for 2–3 talking segments, 5–10 minutes each, with natural stopping points on the water.*

### Wetlands Importance
The wetlands around New Orleans are some of the most ecologically vital in the United States. They:
- Absorb storm surge during hurricanes
- Filter water naturally
- Provide habitat for thousands of species
- Slow and break up hurricanes (especially intact cypress forests)

Louisiana loses a football field of wetland **every hour** due to erosion and subsidence. Talk about local conservation efforts — Common Ground Relief, CPRA, the Manchac restoration history.

### Cypress Trees
Bald cypress trees are recognizable by their wide trunks and "knees" rising from the water. The knees are part of the root system and help stabilize the tree in soft wetland soil.

Some Louisiana cypress trees are hundreds of years old — distant cousins of California redwoods, with roots in the ancient forest before the last ice age.

**Manchac was completely logged.** Most trees you see are relatively young. Talk about:
- Cypressine oil and the logging industry
- How cypress wood built New Orleans (rot-resistant, invaluable for the wet climate)
- What we lost and what's being brought back
- Lessons from clear-cut logging

### Birds
Keep an eye out for:
- Great blue herons
- Egrets
- Ibises
- Limpkin (eater of apple snail eggs)
- Ospreys
- Bald eagles (occasional)
- Barred owls / great horned owls
- Kingfisher
- Woodpeckers

These birds depend on shallow wetland water to hunt fish and small animals.

### Fish & Aquatic Life
- Gar, catfish, bass
- Turtles
- Invasive water hyacinth (talk about its spread and impact)
- Bull tongue — used as a food source traded between Native Americans and French settlers

### Alligators
Alligators live throughout Louisiana wetlands but are typically very shy around humans and prefer to avoid people. Never feed wildlife.

Key talking points:
- Difference between an eco tour (no feeding, no motors) vs. boat tours that interact with wildlife
- Alligators were brought back from near-extinction after a hunting ban in the 1960s–80s
- Add your own alligator facts: brumation (winter dormancy), measuring growth by snout length, etc.

## Emergency Procedures

**Stay calm. Take clear action. Safety always takes priority over completing the route.**

### Lightning or Severe Weather
1. End the tour immediately
2. Guide the group to the nearest safe landing area
3. Get guests off the water quickly and calmly
4. Contact management if necessary

### Capsized Kayak
1. Instruct guest to stay calm
2. Have them hold onto their kayak and paddle
3. Paddle over to assist
4. Help stabilize the kayak
5. Assist guest back in, or guide to shore if needed
6. Check that guest is okay before continuing

### Guest Injury or Medical Issue
1. Stop the group
2. Assess the situation
3. Move group to shore if necessary
4. Provide basic assistance
5. Contact emergency services (911) if needed
6. **Report all incidents to management immediately**
