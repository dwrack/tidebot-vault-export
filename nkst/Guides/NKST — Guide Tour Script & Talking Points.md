# NKST — Guide Tour Script & Talking Points

**New Orleans Kayak Swamp Tours / Nola Kayak Tours**

---

## A Note Before You Read This

This isn't a word-for-word script. If you sound like you're reading from a script, guests will feel it and the tour dies. What this is: a framework. The order matters, the content matters, but the words should come out in your voice. Read this until the concepts are internalized — then put it down and talk to people.

The best guides are genuinely curious about the swamp. If you're faking enthusiasm, guests know. Find the thing about this place that actually interests you, and lead with that.

---

## The Night Before

Text all guests. Keep it short. Something like:

> "Hey, this is [Name] from New Orleans Kayak Swamp Tours — your guide tomorrow. Confirming your [9am / 11:30am / etc.] tour. Meet at **740 N. Rampart St** (the Rampart Treehouse) by [time]. Wear clothes you don't mind getting wet, bring water and sunscreen. See you tomorrow! [Your number]"

Why this matters: guests who get a personal text the night before are calmer, more on time, and more likely to leave a review. It sets you apart from every other tour they've ever taken.

**Always confirm 740 N. Rampart St. explicitly in this text — not just "the pickup location."** Some guests have the old 405 Frenchmen St. address saved from previous bookings or friends' recommendations. If they show up there, they're 15 minutes away and possibly already panicking.

---

## Van Ride: Setting the Stage

This is your first impression on the road. Guests are usually a mix of excited and uncertain — they're not sure what they signed up for. Your job for the first 10 minutes is to calm any anxiety, build anticipation, and start educating without lecturing.

**After everyone's loaded and you're moving:**

"Welcome everyone — I'm [Name], I'll be your guide today. We're heading out to the Maurepas Swamp Wildlife Management Area, about 30 to 40 minutes outside the city near LaPlace. We're going to be paddling in the Manchac Swamp — the second largest bald cypress swamp in the United States."

Let that land. Then:

"Before we get on the water, we'll stop at a gas station — perfect time for a restroom break and to grab anything you need. Once we're at the launch, we'll do a safety briefing, get everyone in their gear, and hit the water. The tour is about two hours on the water, and we'll have a few stops along the way where I'll share some things about the ecology, the history, and whatever wildlife shows up."

**Common van questions and how to handle them:**

- "Will we see alligators?" — "Almost certainly, yes — spring through fall is prime time. I can't guarantee it, but I've never had a tour where we didn't see at least one. What I can guarantee is that we don't feed them or bait them — which is how we're different from the motorized tours out here. The gators on our route are wild and behave naturally."
- "Is it safe?" — "Completely. Alligators in this ecosystem are shy around humans. There's no recorded kayak attack in this area. They'd much rather avoid you than interact with you. The main thing we ask is that you follow the guide's instructions on the water, which we'll go over before we launch."
- "How hard is the paddling?" — "Very manageable. We use sit-on-top tandem kayaks — they're stable and easy to maneuver. No experience needed. The water is calm, there's no current to fight, and we go at a pace that works for everyone in the group."

**Ecology teaser on the drive:**

As you get closer and start seeing cypress along the highway:

"You're starting to see bald cypress — that's the signature tree of the Louisiana swamp. Fun fact: Louisiana contains about 40% of all coastal wetlands in the entire United States. And we're losing them — roughly a football field of coastal land every single hour to erosion and sea level change. The swamp you're about to paddle through isn't just beautiful — it's one of the most ecologically important places in North America."

## Gas Station Stop

Keep it brief and efficient. "We've got about 10 minutes — bathrooms are inside, grab water or snacks if you need anything." You're filling the tank (Shell card, code 6006) or topping off if needed. Regroup everyone before getting back in the van. Do a quick headcount.

---

## At the Launch: Pre-Water Briefing

Get everyone out of the van, waivers checked (QR code on the back of the van seat), PFDs distributed. Do not rush this. The safety briefing is non-negotiable and needs to be delivered clearly every single time.

**Waiver check:**
"Before we get on the water, I need to confirm everyone has completed the digital waiver — you should have gotten a link when you booked. Pull it up on your phone and show me the confirmation screen. If you haven't done it yet, scan this QR code."

**PFD fitting:**
"These are USCG-approved Type III life jackets. Everyone wears one — no exceptions, the whole time you're on the water. It's not a suggestion, it's the law, and it's just smart. Let me show you how to fit it correctly."

Show the adjustment. Check each guest. A PFD that fits right is snug but not restricting — you should be able to take a full breath. If it rides up over their chin when you tug it from the shoulders, it's too loose.

**Paddling demo:**

"Watch me first, then we'll have everyone try it before we push off."

- Forward stroke: blade fully submerged, pull through to your hip, switch sides
- Stopping/braking: paddle flat in the water, hold
- Turning: paddle on one side only; or reverse on the opposite side for a tight turn

"For tandems — the person in the back controls the direction. The person in the front sets the pace. Heavier paddler goes in the back. If you're out of sync, you'll just spin in circles — which happens and it's fine. Just laugh and reset."

**Signals:**

"I'll be using paddle signals on the water to communicate without shouting across the bayou. Here's what they mean:"

- Paddle vertical, held still = "Come to me" (regroup)
- Paddle horizontal = "Stop"
- Paddle at 45 degrees = "Go that direction"
- Paddle vertical, waving = Distress — I need help immediately

"If you ever have a problem, whistle three times. Everyone in this group should be able to hear that."

**Capsize procedure:**

"The kayaks are very stable, but capsizes happen — usually when someone tries to stand up or lean too far. If you go over: stay calm, hold onto your kayak and paddle, don't try to swim to shore. I will come to you. The water is between two and eight feet deep depending on where we are. The kayak will not sink. I'll paddle over, stabilize the boat, and help you back in."

**Final note before launch:**

"Keep the group together — please don't paddle ahead of me. We'll make a few stops where I'll share things about the ecology and history. Keep your hands inside the kayak when we're near the vegetation. Don't touch or feed any wildlife — this is an eco-tour, and that means we watch and we don't interact."

## On the Water: Content Stops

You'll make 2–3 content stops during the tour, each about 5–10 minutes. Raft up or pull alongside something visual — a cypress grove, a wildlife sighting, a patch of water hyacinth. Each stop should feel like a natural pause, not a lecture.

### Stop 1: The Trees and the History

**Set-up:** Pull up to a cypress grove. This works best early in the tour when guests are still orienting.

"These trees you're looking at — bald cypress — are the dominant species of this swamp. They can live for hundreds of years. But almost every tree you're paddling through right now is regrowth. In the early 1900s, logging companies came through this area and took virtually everything. The original-growth cypress was some of the most valuable timber in the South — extremely rot-resistant, which made it perfect for railroad ties, fencing, construction.

They stripped it. What you're seeing is what came back over the last century. That's actually the conservation story here — this swamp wasn't always here. It was almost gone. And now it's back, and it's thriving."

**The knees:**

"These root structures sticking up out of the water — those are cypress knees. For a long time the theory was that they helped the tree breathe in the waterlogged soil. Current research suggests they're actually structural stabilizers — like a root system above the waterline that anchors the tree against wind and water movement. The breathing theory is still in the textbooks, but it's mostly been walked back."

**Tannins:**

"The water looks like dark tea — that's from tannins, organic compounds released as cypress leaves and other plant matter decompose. It's not pollution. The water is actually quite clean. Think of it like steeping tea. It's acidic enough that it doesn't support some of the bacteria you'd worry about in other water. Still, don't drink it."

**Indigenous history:**

"One more thing about this place — the word 'Manchac' is Choctaw. So is 'bayou.' The Choctaw Nation occupied this land for thousands of years before European contact. They knew this ecosystem intimately — traded it, navigated it, built their lives around it. The names they left behind are everywhere in southern Louisiana."

---

### Stop 2: Ecology and the Living System

**Set-up:** Pull up near water hyacinth or open water with visible wildlife.

"What you're looking at here is water hyacinth — that's the plant with the purple flowers and the bulbous stems that float. Beautiful plant. Incredibly destructive. It's an invasive species from South America, introduced in the 1800s at a trade exposition in New Orleans — someone thought it was pretty and put it in a pond. Now it's all over the Gulf Coast.

The problem is it grows fast enough to form dense mats that block sunlight from the water below, deplete oxygen, and crowd out native plants. Fish populations suffer. Waterfowl can't access the water. It's a real problem.

Here's the silver lining: in winter, it dies back significantly. And when that happens, we get access to parts of this bayou that are off-limits in spring and summer. Deeper channels open up. The view changes completely."

**Louisiana wetlands:**

"Louisiana has 40% of the coastal wetlands in the entire United States. That's not because Louisiana is particularly big — it's because the Mississippi River delta has been building this landscape for thousands of years. The problem is that the same levee system that protects New Orleans from flooding has cut off the sediment supply that keeps these wetlands alive. Without new sediment, the land subsides, saltwater intrudes, and the wetlands disappear. A football field per hour. That's not a metaphor — that's the measured rate."

**Bull tongue:**

If you see bull tongue — the tall emergent plant with arrow-shaped leaves — point it out:

"That plant is called bull tongue. Both Native Americans and French colonists used the root as a trade item — it's edible, starchy, basically a wild potato. If you're ever stranded in a Louisiana swamp with a fire and some salt, that's your dinner."

---

### Stop 3: Wildlife

**Set-up:** When you've spotted something worth stopping for — alligator, great blue heron, anhinga, osprey — or simply at a good visual spot for the wrap-up.

**Alligators (if present):**

"There's one. See the snout — that dark line in the water over there? Here's the trick for estimating size: the distance between the snout and the eye, measured in inches, roughly equals the body length in feet. So if snout to eye is about 8 inches, that's probably a 7 or 8 foot gator.

Alligators were hunted nearly to extinction through the 1960s and 1970s. Louisiana and Florida banned hunting in 1962. The recovery since then is one of the greatest conservation success stories in American wildlife history. They went from near-extinction to a stable population of millions in about 40 years.

One thing I'll ask: don't try to approach or splash toward it. Not because it's dangerous — it almost certainly won't react — but because habituating wild animals to humans is how they lose their wariness and end up in situations that get them killed. We keep our distance and watch."

**Birds:**

"We're in one of the most productive bird habitats in North America. What you might see today includes great blue herons — that massive slate-blue bird — and great egrets, the tall white ones. The smaller white birds are typically snowy egrets. The dark bird drying its wings on that branch is an anhinga — sometimes called the 'snakebird' because it swims with its body submerged and just its neck out.

If you see a brilliant orange-yellow bird about the size of your fist in the shrubs — that's a prothonotary warbler. They're sometimes called the golden swamp warbler and they're special to see. And if we're very lucky, roseate spoonbills — pink birds with a spatula-shaped bill. They're rare enough that when you see one, the whole boat cheers."

## Handling Awkward Situations

### Guest refuses to wear PFD

Don't make it a confrontation. Be matter-of-fact:

"I hear you — they're not the most comfortable thing in the world. But this is a non-negotiable on our tours — it's the law on the water, it's our insurance, and it's my responsibility. I can't put you in a kayak without it. What I can do is adjust it so it fits as comfortably as possible."

If they continue to refuse after that: they don't get in a kayak. Full stop. Explain calmly that you'd be happy to contact the office about next steps but you cannot proceed. This is never worth the risk — to them or to the business.

### Guest is visibly scared

This is more common than you'd think. Don't minimize it. Acknowledge it.

"It's completely normal to feel nervous before getting in a kayak if you haven't done it before. These boats are very stable — much harder to tip than they look. What I'd suggest is we get you in the kayak at the dock, just sitting still, before we go anywhere. Get a feel for it. And then we'll push off slowly. You're in control of your own boat. If at any point you want to stop, we stop."

For guests who really cannot do it: you can always offer to paddle alongside them closely, or in extreme cases, bring them back to the launch and wait with the van while the rest of the group continues — but only if you have enough experience to assess that the group can manage safely. When in doubt, contact management.

### Someone asks to feed wildlife

"I completely understand the impulse — it's a natural thing to want to connect. But on an eco-tour, we have a hard rule about feeding wildlife, and it comes from respect for the animal, not rules for the sake of rules. A fed alligator loses its wariness of humans. When that happens, it becomes what wildlife managers call 'a problem animal' — and problem animals get removed. Feeding an alligator is, in a weird way, signing its death warrant. So we watch, we appreciate, and we leave them exactly as we found them."

Same logic applies to birds and any other wildlife.

### Someone asks a question you don't know the answer to

Just say so. Seriously. Guests respect honesty far more than a confident wrong answer.

"That's a great question — I don't know the answer to that off the top of my head. I'll find out and send you a note if you want to leave me your email. What I do know is..."

Then pivot to something you do know that's related.

### Tense dynamic in a group (conflict between guests, unhappy guest)

Address it quietly and directly. If there's friction in a tandem kayak — common in couples — give them a moment at a content stop and offer some low-key coaching on paddling in sync. Most tandem frustration is just technique. Keep it light: "It took me a while to get the rhythm too."

If a guest is openly unhappy about the experience (didn't see an alligator, tour isn't what they expected), acknowledge their disappointment genuinely and pivot to what you can offer right now. Don't apologize for wildlife not cooperating — it's a wild ecosystem. Do stay warm and engaged. A guide who stays present and generous usually wins people over by the end.

## Return and Wrap-Up

Equipment collection first — paddles, PFDs, kayaks secured. Then gather the group.

"That's the Manchac Swamp. [Something specific you saw or talked about today — make it personal to the tour.] Thank you genuinely for coming out with us today."

**The review ask:**

"One quick thing before we head back — if you had a good time today, the single most helpful thing you can do for a small local tour company like ours is leave us a review on Google or TripAdvisor. It takes about 90 seconds and it makes a real difference. I'll drop the link in a text to everyone."

Do not be awkward about this. You earned it if the tour was good.

**On tipping in New Orleans:**

You don't need to say this explicitly, but if guests ask about tipping culture — or if you want to frame it:

"Tipping culture in New Orleans is pretty much what you'd expect from a city that runs on hospitality. If you had a good time and want to take care of your guide, it's always appreciated — but never expected."

If guests ask where to tip directly (cash vs. app): take whatever is offered graciously. Cash is great. Some guests will tip through FareHarbor. Either is fine.

## Situations That Come Up (Real Calls & Texts From 12 Years of Data)

The following scenarios are drawn directly from 12 years of customer calls and staff texts. They are real situations that have happened — sometimes multiple times. Know how to handle them before they happen to you.

---

### 1. Guest Can't Find You at Pickup

This happens more than you'd think. A guest is circling the block, standing in front of the wrong building, or showed up at the old address (405 Frenchmen St.).

**What to do:** Keep your phone visible and check it during the entire pickup window — not just at departure time. If someone texts saying they can't find you, respond immediately and stay put. They are almost always within one block.

If a guest mentions "Frenchmen Street" or "French Quarter": they have the old address. Our pickup is at **740 N. Rampart St. (Rampart Treehouse)**. Give them directions from wherever they are, and if they're close, stay on text with them until they're in front of you.

Never leave the pickup location before departure time just because most guests are there. The last one matters.

---

### 2. Guest Calls or Shows Up Day-Of to Add Transportation

If a guest arrives at the launch without a shuttle reservation and expects or needs a ride back, or calls you day-of to ask about adding the shuttle:

**What to do:** Do not turn them away or make a unilateral call. Contact management immediately. You do not have FareHarbor access to add or charge for transportation. The answer from management is usually yes, there's space — but the payment and booking update happens through the office, not through you.

Tell the guest: "Let me check with our office on that right now — I want to make sure we have space for you." Then call or text management. Do not promise anything until you have confirmation.

---

### 3. You're the Only Guide and Only One Person Booked

Minimum participation decisions are not yours to make. If you're scheduled for an extended tour and you're looking at a near-empty manifest the afternoon before:

**What to do:** If management hasn't contacted you by 5pm the day before about a thin booking, text or call them. Don't assume the tour is running just because no one has said otherwise. Ask. Management may have already decided to cancel and just hasn't looped you in yet.

Never cancel a tour yourself. Never tell a guest their tour is cancelled without management authorization.

---

### 4. You Need to Switch Tours (Manchac to Honey Island or Vice Versa)

If a tour location is being switched due to water levels, participation, or other operational reasons, management will have already texted guests before tour day. That communication is management's job, not yours.

**What to do:** If you arrive at pickup and guests are expecting a different location than what you're taking them to, and management has not yet communicated this — call management before you do anything. Do not tell guests the tour switched without management authorization. There may be reasons you're unaware of, and a guest who expected Manchac and finds out at the van that they're going to Honey Island (or vice versa) deserves an explanation that comes from a complete picture.

If management has already communicated the switch, guests will likely bring it up themselves — confirm it, explain why briefly, and move forward positively.

---

### 5. Guest Asks About Minimum Age on the Spot

A parent with a young child asks: "Is my kid old enough for this?"

**What to do:** Tell guests that all children must paddle tandem with an adult guardian. If the child is clearly 8 or older, confirm they're welcome. If a parent asks specifically about a child under 8, give them the honest answer: "Let me check with management on that one to make sure we set you up safely — I want to give you the right answer." Then contact management.

Do not make up or improvise an age policy in the field. This one has legal implications if something goes wrong. When in doubt, escalate.

---

### 6. Guest Mentions a Hurricane Is Coming

If a guest brings up hurricane activity on or before a tour day:

**What to do:** If there's a named storm forecast and management has not yet cancelled the tour, call management immediately. Do not make a hurricane cancellation call yourself — that decision belongs to management and the owner. You may have incomplete information about the storm track, and cancellation involves refund processing and multiple guest notifications that happen through FareHarbor and GHL.

If a tour is running and a guest asks about a storm: your honest answer is "Management monitors the forecast closely and will contact everyone proactively if we need to cancel. If you've heard something and haven't received a message from us, let me check in with the office right now." Then contact management.

---

### 7. Guest Can't Add the Shuttle at the Booking Screen

If a guest reaches you (before tour day) saying they couldn't figure out how to add transportation online:

**What to do:** Tell them to text **504-571-9975** and the office will add it manually. You don't have FareHarbor edit permissions to do this yourself — don't try. The office handles all booking modifications.

If it's the night before or day-of: contact management directly and relay the guest's request. Management will verify space is available and handle the booking update.
