# Location Guide — Honey Island Swamp
*New Orleans Kayak Swamp Tours | Guide Training Reference*
*Last updated: March 2026*

> This document covers everything specific to operating at the Honey Island Swamp / Pearl River launch. Read the Guide SOP Master first — this doc assumes you know the standard day-of procedure and covers only what is different or location-specific here.

---

## Overview

Honey Island Swamp is a Pearl River basin wetland straddling the Louisiana-Mississippi state line. It is the most ecologically diverse of our three tour locations and the most remote. The drive is longer, the route options change significantly with water level, and the location has its own logistics that differ from Manchac. Know this doc before your first solo tour here.

---

## Guest Pickup Location

**740 N. Rampart St. (Rampart Treehouse)** — all Honey Island tours pick up here. No exceptions.

---

## Driving to the Launch

**Do NOT stop at the Riverside Travel gas station en route.** On Honey Island tours, the van drives directly to the Pit Stop Bar at the launch area. The Pit Stop handles the restroom and water stop for this location — do not split that stop between two locations.

**Key turns:** There are two very sharp turns on the route to Pearl River. Know them before your first solo. Practice the route during a shadow shift. The van with a trailer does not handle these turns like a regular vehicle.

**Drive time:** Approximately 45–60 minutes from pickup depending on traffic.

---

## Arrival: Pit Stop Bar

When you arrive at the launch area, the **first stop is the Pit Stop Bar**:

- **Bathrooms:** Located inside the Pit Stop Bar. Direct all guests here before gearing up.
- **Water:** Available at the Pit Stop. Encourage guests to grab water here, especially in summer.
- After guests have used the restroom and grabbed water, direct them to the gear area: *"Grab a PFD and a paddle and wait by the water for the rest of the group."*

There are no other public restrooms at this launch. If guests miss the Pit Stop, they are waiting until the end of the tour.

---

## Equipment Storage

**Trailer:** Stored at the gas station near the launch — not at 1407 Piety St. Confirm trailer location with management before your first solo tour here. If the trailer is not at the expected location, contact management immediately — do not improvise.

**Kayaks and gear:** Loaded from the trailer at the launch. Set up kayaks and paddles on the grass. Give yourself a maximum of 15 minutes to unload and stage gear.

**PFD care:** After every Honey Island tour, hang all PFDs on the clothesline to dry. Spray the inside of each PFD with MiraZyme before the next tour. PFDs must not produce a foul odor — check them before every guest interaction.

**Cleaning:** Pressure wash kayaks and equipment on Tues/Wed weekly. After each tour, turn boats around and clear trash, sticks, and excess water from the hulls.

## Water Level & Route Selection

**Check the gauge the night before AND the morning of every Honey Island tour:**
**wx.aerisweather.com/rivers/gauge/PERL1.html**

| Gauge Reading | Condition | Route |
|---|---|---|
| Under 5 ft | Extreme low | Push tour to Manchac; or Gum Creek Pearl River (reduced group size) |
| 5–8 ft | Summer low (Aug/Sep — Jackson MS dam closed) | Honey Island to Brockham Bayou (~4.5 miles) |
| 8–12 ft | Normal operating range | Upper Honey Island (~3.5 mi) or Lower Honey Island (~5 mi) |
| Over 12 ft | Extreme high (late winter/spring) | Push tour to Manchac; Gum Creek only for physically fit groups |

**Important:** Route changes are a management decision. If the gauge requires a reroute, notify management as early as possible — ideally the night before. Management communicates route changes to guests. Do not inform guests of a route change without management confirmation.

The Pearl River water level is affected by the Jackson, MS dam. Summer levels (Aug–Sep) are typically lower because the dam is closed. This opens the Brockham Bayou route that isn't always accessible.

## Launch Procedure

1. Drop guests at the Pit Stop Bar for restrooms and water
2. Unload kayaks, paddles, and gear on the grass (max 15 minutes)
3. Check PFDs for odor and condition
4. Clean tables and counters of any chicken waste or previous tour debris — pick up trash in the parking lot, empty the garbage can
5. Ensure the hose is returned and turned off when done
6. Fit guests with PFDs at the water's edge
7. Deliver safety briefing and paddle demo (see SOP Master, sections 4.5–4.6)
8. Show guests the route on the map — point out the two swamp school stops and the regroup points; ask stronger paddlers to remember the regroup spots

### Honey Island to Brockham Bayou (~4.5 miles) — Low Water Route
- Used when gauge is 5–8 ft
- More sheltered, narrower channels
- Good for wildlife viewing in tighter vegetation

## Location-Specific Talking Points

This location has distinct content that differs from Manchac. See the Honey Island Spiel section in the SOP Master (Part 6) for full detail. Key topics specific to this location:

**Honey Island Swamp Monster:** Louisiana's most famous cryptid, reportedly sighted here in the 1960s by a wildlife biologist. Never confirmed, never fully debunked. Works well as a hook at the start of the tour.

**Pearl River geography:** The Pearl River forms the Louisiana-Mississippi state line and drains a large basin upstream. The Jackson, MS dam controls flow and directly affects our water levels — explaining this to guests makes the gauge system feel real and interesting rather than arbitrary.

**Freshwater mussels:** Look for shells on muddy banks. One of the most endangered animal groups in North America; their presence indicates clean water. Guests are usually surprised that something so inconspicuous matters so much ecologically.

**Logging history:** The Pearl River basin was heavily logged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Old logging canals are still visible in the swamp. The forest has recovered, but old-growth cypress is essentially gone.

**Salt water encroachment:** This freshwater system is threatened as sea levels rise and human alterations to river flow reduce freshwater input. When cypress die from salt intrusion, you get ghost forests — visible near the coast.

## End of Tour

- Collect all PFDs and paddles; load kayaks
- Return guests to van
- Drive back to city; use drive to continue conversation, give restaurant recommendations, build toward the review ask at drop-off
- **Do not stop at Riverside Travel on the return either** unless fueling is necessary — fill up at a Shell station on the route back. Card code 6006. Save receipt.
- Return to 1407 Piety St: hang PFDs, spray with MiraZyme, lock base, return keys
