# New Orleans Kayak Swamp Tours — SOP: Pre-Tour Preparation
*2026-03-27 | Vessel type: kayak/paddle vessel*

> **Purpose:** Ensure every tour launches safely, legally, and professionally. This SOP covers everything from the day before through launch.

---

## ⏰ Day-Before Checklist

- [ ] Confirm all bookings in FareHarbor — check headcount, any special requests
- [ ] Check weather forecast — wind, precipitation, wave height (if applicable)
- [ ] Send 48-hour reminder email to all confirmed guests (see Guest Comms SOP)
- [ ] Confirm staff/guide availability and assignments
- [ ] Review float plan / tour route for any new conditions
- [ ] Verify all gear is staged, charged, and accounted for

## 🌅 Morning-Of Checklist (Staff Arrival)

- [ ] All kayaks/SUPs off rack, inspected for hull damage
- [ ] Paddles counted and assigned (1 spare per 10 guests)
- [ ] PFDs counted and sorted by size — all USCG-approved Type III
- [ ] Spray skirts (if applicable) inspected
- [ ] Bilge pumps, paddle floats, throw bags — all present
- [ ] First aid kit stocked and accessible
- [ ] Safety whistle on every guide
- [ ] Communication device (VHF radio or cell phone) on guide
- [ ] Launch site clear and signage in place

## 👋 Guest Check-In Procedure

- [ ] Greet guests by name when possible
- [ ] Verify headcount against booking manifest
- [ ] Collect signed liability waiver (digital preferred — DocuSeal/FareHarbor)
- [ ] Fit PFDs — guide checks every guest individually
- [ ] Safety briefing delivered (see script below)
- [ ] Confirm any medical/physical limitations
- [ ] Photo opportunity at launch (social media + guest memory)

## 📢 Safety Briefing Script

> *"Welcome, everyone! Before we head out, I want to cover a few safety points. Your life jacket must be worn at all times on the water — it's required by law and it's what keeps you safe. Stay within sight of your guide at all times. If you flip, stay calm — your kayak will float. Paddle your way back and we'll help you re-enter. If you hear two short whistle blasts, stop paddling and look to your guide. Any questions before we launch?"*

## ☁️ Weather Hold Protocol

**Decision maker:** Guide lead / Manager on duty

| Condition | Action |
|---|---|
| Wind > 15mph sustained | Hold — reschedule |
| Lightning within 10 miles | Cancel / shelter immediately |
| Visibility < 0.25 mile | Hold |
| Water temp < 50°F (early/late season) | Require wetsuit or cancel |

**Weather hold communication:** Use guest communication template in Guest Comms SOP

## 📝 Post-Tour Checklist

- [ ] All gear returned, rinsed, and stored
- [ ] Incident report filed if any injury/incident occurred
- [ ] Guest count confirmed out
- [ ] Review request triggered in GHL (auto if connected, else manual SMS)
- [ ] Any feedback noted for manager coaching log

Bayou Wildlife & Ecology Talking Points
Optional facts guides can share during the tour
Guides are encouraged to share interesting information about the local ecosystem to enhance the guest experience. This is meant to be a framework to build your own tour around. Opt to share wetland facts, history of the logging in the manchac swamp which can be found online, history of the land originally occupied by native americans (manchac and bayou are both choctaw words), facts about cypress and tupelo forests- very little still in existence, facts about wildlife and birds. Have about 2-3 main talking segments 5-10 minutes each. Breaking the tour into two or three stopping points.
Wetlands Importance
The wetlands around New Orleans are some of the most important ecosystems in the United States.
They help:
* absorb storm surge during hurricanes
* filter water naturally
* provide habitat for thousands of species
* Slow the speed and break up hurricanes (especially intact cypress forests)
Louisiana loses wetlands every year due to erosion and subsidence, which makes conservation efforts extremely important. We lose a football field every hour. Talk about local conservation efforts.
________________


Cypress Trees
Many bayous are filled with bald cypress trees. These trees are easy to recognize by their wide trunks and “knees” sticking up from the water. Cypress knees are part of the root system and help stabilize the tree in soft wetland soil.
Some cypress trees in Louisiana are hundreds of years old. Cousins of redwoods. Ancient trees before the last ice age. Manchac swamp was completely logged, most trees are young. Talk about cypressine oil and the logging history of Manchac and lessons learned from clear cut logging. Importance to New Orleans as building material that is rot resistant etc. Research!
________________


Birds
The wetlands are home to a huge variety of bird species including:
* Great blue herons
* Egrets
* Ibises
* Limpkin (eater of apple snail eggs)
* Ospreys
* Bald eagles (occasionally)
* Barred Owls
* Occasional Great Horned Owls
* Kingfisher
* Woodpeckers
These birds rely on the shallow waters of the wetlands to hunt for fish and small animals.

The bayou ecosystem supports many fish and aquatic species including:
* gar
* catfish

* Turtles
* Invasive plant species (hyacinth)
* Research wetland plants (bull tongue used as a potato for trading between native americans and french)

Alligators
Alligators live throughout Louisiana wetlands.
They are typically very shy around humans and prefer to avoid people.
Guides should always keep a respectful distance and never feed wildlife.
Difference between an eco tour that does not feed gators or use motors vs boat tours.
Alligators brought back from near extinction from hunting ban in the 60s-80s.
Add your favorite alligator facts. (brumation, growth measured with snout, etc)

Emergency Procedures
Guides must stay calm and take clear action if needed.
Lightning or Severe Weather
If lightning or severe weather approaches:
1. End the tour immediately
2. Guide the group to the nearest safe landing area
3. Get guests off the water as quickly and calmly as possible
4. Contact management if necessary
Safety always takes priority over completing the tour route.
________________


Capsized Kayak
If a guest flips their kayak:
1. Instruct them to stay calm
2. Have them hold onto their kayak and paddle
3. Paddle over to assist
4. Help stabilize the kayak
5. Assist the guest back into the kayak or guide them to shore if needed
Always check that the guest is okay before continuing.
