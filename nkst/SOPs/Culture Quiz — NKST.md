# How We Run Things — The Quiz
### New Orleans Kayak Swamp Tours

*For new hires. Read each scenario. Pick your gut answer. Then read the takeaway.*

*Managers: walk through these together. The conversation matters more than the "score."*

---

### Scenario 1: The Local Connection

A bartender friend at a restaurant on Magazine Street mentions their staff has never been out to the swamp. You've been meaning to pitch the local business outreach program but nobody told you exactly how to do it.

**What do you do?**

A) Wait until your manager sets up the official program
B) Tell your friend about it casually and let it die there
C) Bring it up in the Slack channel: "Hey, I've got a contact at [restaurant]. They're interested in a staff outing. What's our move?"

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Own Your Patch.** Option C. You don't need a finished program to make a connection. You need to surface the opportunity so the team can act on it. That bartender's staff could fill a Tuesday afternoon that was going to be empty. The guides who live in this city ARE the sales team, whether that's in the job description or not.
</details>

---

### Scenario 2: The Marshmallow Question

A guest on your tour asks, "Do we get to feed the alligators?" They saw another tour company advertising it. They seem genuinely excited about it.

**What do you do?**

A) Say "No, we don't do that" and move on
B) Explain why we don't, what it does to the ecosystem, and what we do instead: "We show you the real swamp. These gators are wild. That's what makes this different."
C) Trash the other tour company by name

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real.** Option B. You don't need to attack anyone else. You just need to be honest about why we do it differently and let the experience speak for itself. Most guests who hear the real reason are genuinely glad they chose us. That bartender's tourist is going to remember the guide who taught them something, not the one who tossed marshmallows.
</details>

---

### Scenario 3: The Waiver Rush

You're at the launch point. The van is loaded. One couple says they already signed the waiver online but you can't find their names in the system. They're getting impatient. The rest of the group is ready to go. It's hot.

**What do you do?**

A) Let them on. They say they signed it and you're already running late.
B) Have them re-sign on your phone right there. Takes 2 minutes. "Sorry about the tech glitch, this'll be quick."
C) Leave them behind and take the rest of the group

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job.** Option B. No signed waiver, no tour. But you don't have to be a jerk about it. Blame the technology, make it fast, keep the energy up. Two minutes of awkwardness now vs. a liability nightmare later isn't even a real choice. And leaving them behind is just bad service when there's a simple fix.
</details>

---

### Scenario 4: The Senior Guide's Silence

You notice that one of the more experienced guides has been showing up late, not cleaning the van after tours, and generally seeming checked out. Nobody's said anything because this person has been here for 4 years and everyone likes them. You're newer.

**What do you do?**

A) Keep your head down. You're new, it's not your place.
B) Talk to them one-on-one: "Hey, you good? Noticed you've been a little off lately. Anything I can help with?"
C) Go straight to the manager

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Respect the Work.** Option B is the human move. Peer-to-peer, private, from a place of genuine concern. They might be going through something. They might not realize how it's landing. Either way, someone needs to say something before it becomes a real problem. If nothing changes after that conversation, then loop in the manager. Tenure doesn't equal immunity from the basics.
</details>

---

### Scenario 5: The Captain's Log

End of a great tour. Everyone's vibing on the drive back. You had a first-timer who was terrified of kayaking but ended up loving it. A guest spotted a barred owl, which almost never happens. One of the experienced paddlers asked about a route variation you'd never considered.

**What do you do?**

A) Enjoy the drive and head home
B) Drop a quick captain's log in the channel: owl sighting, the nervous first-timer's breakthrough moment, the route idea from the experienced paddler
C) Text your manager a summary later tonight

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Get It Out of Your Head.** Option B. That owl sighting goes in the channel so the next guide can mention it: "We've been seeing a barred owl out here lately." The first-timer's story becomes content gold. The route idea gets discussed by the team. None of that happens if it stays in your head. Voice memo, typed message, photo. Doesn't matter. Get it captured while it's fresh.
</details>

## How'd You Do?

No grade. No pass/fail. The point is getting these five things in your head before your first paddle so they're already running when the real situations hit.

If you disagreed with anything, bring it up. The best guides push back.
