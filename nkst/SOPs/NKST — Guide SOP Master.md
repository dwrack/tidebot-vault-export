## A Note on This Document

This SOP consolidates guidance from multiple documents written over ten years of operation. Where practices have evolved, the current standard is marked **[CURRENT]** and the older practice is noted for context. When in doubt, follow the most recent version and ask management.

This document is designed to be your complete reference as a new guide preparing for your first solo tour. Read it in full before your first shadow shift. Review it before every tour until the procedures are instinctive.

### What Will Disqualify You

- History of violent or sexually inappropriate conduct
- Inability to pass a basic background check

## Part 2: Training Progression

### Phase 1: Shadow Tours (2-3 tours minimum)
- Ride along and observe an experienced guide running a full tour
- Take notes on the route, the briefing, the content stops, the return procedure
- Practice loading and unloading kayaks
- Practice backing the trailer (this takes time — don't skip it)

### Phase 2: Co-Guide Tours (1-2 tours)
- Co-guide with an experienced guide; you lead segments while they observe
- Lead the safety briefing and paddle demo at least once
- Handle the FareHarbor check-in while the senior guide observes
- Practice the paddle signal demonstration

### Phase 3: First Solo Tour
- Complete before solo: sign the contractor agreement; receive FareHarbor login; confirm CPR cert is current
- First solo tour should have management on call and available by phone throughout
- Debrief with management after the first solo tour

### Ongoing Development
- Attend any scheduled guide meetings
- Read updates to this SOP when issued
- Report equipment issues, safety incidents, and near-misses to management immediately — no exceptions

### 4.3 — En Route to Launch (~35–45 min drive)

- [ ] Confirm everyone is buckled
- [ ] No personal phone use while driving — ever
- [ ] Tell guests upfront: the drive is 35–45 minutes and there will be a restroom stop at the launch
- [ ] Use drive time productively: introduce yourself, preview the tour, begin storytelling to build anticipation

**Drive talking points (outbound):**
- Lake Pontchartrain — scale, ecology, the causeway
- The old amusement park (Pontchartrain Beach)
- The castle structure built for the 1984 World's Fair
- What they might see on the water today: alligators, herons, eagles, otters
- Why cypress trees have "knees" and what the swamp smells like (and why)
- Sell merchandise if interest comes up naturally: waterproof phone cases, sunglass straps, t-shirts — can be added to their FareHarbor reservation

**Drive talking points (return):**
- Restaurant and neighborhood recommendations — guests almost always ask
- Thank them and ask them to double-check that they haven't left anything in the van or in their PFD
- Build toward the review ask you'll make at drop-off

### 4.4 — Arrival at Launch

**Location:** Maurepas Swamp WMA, near LaPlace, LA 70068
**Self-drive GPS:** 5H64+W3, LaPlace, LA 70068
**Note:** The trailer is stored at the gas station near the launch — not at Piety St. Confirm with management if you're unsure of trailer location.

- [ ] Back trailer across the street to the launch — **slowly and carefully.** Practice this before your first solo tour. No rushing. Two turns on the route are very sharp — know them before your first solo run
- [ ] **Customer parking:** guests do not park in the motorized boat area. Direct them to the correct area
- [ ] Send guests to the Pit Stop bar for restrooms and water before gearing up — tell them after they've done that, grab a PFD and paddle and wait for the group
- [ ] Unload kayaks and paddles onto the grass. This should take no more than 15 minutes
- [ ] Keep the launch area clean: pick up trash, empty the garbage can, clean tables and counters
- [ ] Ensure the hose is returned and off when finished
- [ ] Check trailer straps and hitch before departure — every time
- [ ] Lock trailer wheels before driving

**PFD Fitting:**
- [ ] Fit every guest with a PFD (USCG Type III). Most PFDs are XXL — show guests how to loosen straps first, then cinch down to a snug fit
- [ ] A properly fitted PFD cannot be pulled over the head
- [ ] For larger guests, offer the triangle-style PFD — it's more comfortable. Use the phrasing *"this one will be more comfortable"* rather than *"here's a larger one"*
- [ ] Check that every PFD has an attached whistle
- [ ] Conduct safety briefing (see 4.5)
- [ ] Paddle demonstration and signal practice (see 4.6)

**PFD Odor Check:** Before fitting any guest, confirm PFDs do **not** have a foul odor. If they do, flag it to management immediately. PFDs must be hung to dry and sprayed with MiraZyme after every single tour — this is not optional.

**Launch Technique:**
1. Place kayak bow-first at the water's edge on the grass
2. Stabilize the kayak by sitting on it; give the first guest a hand to get in, then scoot the boat toward the water
3. Give a second hand to the second guest; lift the boat gently into the water — warn guests it will feel unstable during this transition
4. Adjust seats and foot position once guests are in the water; demonstrate the swivel-sit and how to keep eyes forward, not looking down at the water
5. Tell guests to stay along shore and wait for the full group before paddling — this saves their energy and keeps the group together at the start

### 4.5 — Safety Briefing

Deliver the safety briefing on shore before any guest touches a kayak. Cover all of the following in a warm, conversational tone — not a legal recitation:

1. Life jackets are on for the **entire time we're on the water.** No exceptions. "It's not about whether you can swim — it's about what happens if you're underwater and disoriented."
2. These are sit-on-top kayaks. They don't sink. If you fall off, stay calm, hold onto the kayak and paddle, and I'll be there in seconds.
3. We stay together as a group. If you fall behind, call out and I'll come back.
4. Wildlife: we observe, we never approach, we **never feed.** Not a snack, not a granola bar, nothing. Ever.
5. Weather: if you see lightning, we get off the water immediately. No debate.
6. Questions? Ask anytime.

### 4.6 — Paddle Demonstration & Signals

Demonstrate paddle technique (basic forward stroke, turning, stopping, paddling backwards) and ensure every guest has tried it before entering the water.

**Body position coaching:** Many guests — especially men — tense up their upper body. Stress that their shoulders should stay low and relaxed, hips loose. Each part of the body should be able to move independently, not as one rigid unit. A relaxed body handles the water's movement far better than a stiff one.

**Things to flag in the demo:**
- Branch fishing lines in the water — watch paddles
- Snakes: rare, but if one falls in the boat, use the paddle blade to scoop it out calmly
- Alligators: stop paddling if you see one. Let them have an escape route. They will move.

Demonstrate and have guests repeat the four paddle signals:

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Paddle held vertical, static | Come to me |
| Paddle held horizontal | Stop |
| Paddle held at 45° right or left | Go that direction |
| Paddle held vertical, waving | Distress — emergency |
| Three whistle blasts | Help needed |

"If you see me do any of these, respond immediately. They exist so we can communicate across the water without shouting."

**If you fall out of the kayak:**
"Go to opposite sides of the kayak and take turns trying to pull yourself back on. Go slowly — it's easier than it looks. If you can't get back in, stay with the boat, blow your whistle, and I'll come to you."

**Boat assignment (do this before guests get in the water):**
- **Tandems (Rambler or Tribe 13.5):** Heavier person in the back. Max combined weight ~400 lbs. Buddy up stronger paddlers with weaker ones in tandem boats
- **Singles:** Use for odd group sizes or when you have extra boats and a clearly strong paddler
- **Lead and sweep:** Appoint a lead boat (strong paddler who sets the pace but watches the group) and a sweep boat (strong paddler who stays just behind the slowest guest). Don't get so far ahead of the group that you lose sight of your sweep

**Tour is challenge by choice:** Tell guests: if at any point they don't want to continue, they can let you know and you'll get them to shore. Ask upfront: *"Is anyone here not the strongest swimmer? Please let me know now."*

### 4.7 — On the Water

- [ ] Keep the group together at all times — no stragglers
- [ ] Set a comfortable pace; check on slower paddlers without drawing attention to them
- [ ] Plan 2-3 content stops of 5-10 minutes each — drift, observe, tell the story
- [ ] Be attentive to signs of fatigue, sun exposure, or anxiety — check in proactively
- [ ] Point out wildlife when you see it; let silence work for you — the swamp speaks
- [ ] If a guest is struggling, address it privately and practically: "Want to try adjusting your grip? Here, let me show you."
- [ ] In hot weather: watch for heat fatigue. If someone seems off, get them water and paddle them toward shade
- [ ] In cold weather: watch for early signs of hypothermia — confusion, slurred speech, uncontrolled shivering. Get them to shore and warm them up
- [ ] Never leave a guest alone on the water

**Keep guests engaged:** Guests default to passive. Think of yourself as the conductor and them as your symphony. Get them involved — let them take photos of wildlife, have them help with a task, call their attention to something specific. That said: read the group. Some guests prefer a quiet paddle. Less is more for that crowd.

**Content stops — use the present moment:** A good guide uses what's actually in front of them (a specific animal, an interesting tree, a strange smell) to build into the spiel naturally. Don't recite. Converse.

**Photo opportunity (plan ~10 minutes):** Find the most picturesque spot — open water, cypress backdrop, good light. Get the group set up:
- Paddles pointing the same direction
- Light on guests' faces, not in their eyes
- No other kayakers in the background — just them and the swamp
This becomes your best marketing asset when they post it. Take your time here.

**Show them on the map at the start:** Before leaving the launch, show guests on the map where you're going and where the two "swamp school" stops will be. Tell stronger paddlers to pay attention to the regroup spots so they know where to wait.

**Route decision:** Confirm the route before launch based on the water gauge reading (check night before AND morning of):
- **Under 5 ft** → Manchac or Gum Creek Pearl River (reduced group)
- **5–8 ft** → Honey Island to Brockham Bayou (~4.5 miles)
- **8–12 ft** → Upper Honey Island (~3.5 mi) or Lower Honey Island (~5 mi)
- **Over 12 ft** → Manchac; Gum Creek for groups with physically fit participants only

## Part 6: Wildlife & Ecology Talking Points

These are conversation-starters and depth points for content stops. Adapt to your audience and what you're actually seeing. Don't recite — converse.

### Louisiana's Coastal Wetlands
- Louisiana contains approximately **40% of all U.S. coastal wetlands**
- The coast loses **a football field of land every hour** to erosion, subsidence, and sea level rise
- The swamps buffer New Orleans and surrounding communities from storm surge — they are literal infrastructure
- The Maurepas Swamp WMA is the **second-largest bald cypress swamp in the United States**

### Bald Cypress
- The bald cypress is a cousin of the California redwood — one of the ancient lineages of trees
- "Knees" are woody projections from the roots that help stabilize the tree in waterlogged, oxygen-poor soil
- Cypress wood is naturally rot-resistant — it built much of historic New Orleans, from foundations to fences
- **Manchac was completely clear-cut in the early 20th century.** Every tree you see here is regrowth. That's both sobering and hopeful — the swamp can recover if given the chance.

### Choctaw Heritage
- The word **"Manchac"** is derived from Choctaw — it means "rear entrance" or "back way in"
- The word **"bayou"** is also Choctaw — referring to a slow-moving body of water
- The Choctaw people originally occupied and navigated this entire region; their geographic knowledge shaped the language of the landscape we paddle through today

### Alligators
- American alligators were nearly hunted to extinction in Louisiana by the **1960s**
- After state and federal protection in the 1970s and '80s, the population **fully recovered** — from fewer than 100,000 to over 1.5 million in Louisiana today
- This is one of the few unambiguous conservation success stories in American history
- Alligators are **apex predators** that regulate the entire food web of the swamp; without them, the ecosystem collapses
- They are naturally **fearful of humans** and avoid contact unless fed or provoked
- We **never feed or bait wildlife.** Ever. Not even to guarantee a guest a better photo.

### Honey Island Spiel (5–7 min content stop)

Use this at a natural stopping point on the Honey Island route:

- **Geography:** Honey Island Swamp sits in the Pearl River basin, straddling the Louisiana-Mississippi state line. The Pearl River is a working river with a dam upstream in Jackson, MS — when they release water in summer (Aug/Sep), water levels rise; that's why routes shift seasonally
- **Honey Island name:** Named for the honeybees that used to swarm the island
- **Swamp Monster legend:** The Honey Island Swamp Monster is Louisiana's answer to Bigfoot — a large, bipedal creature reportedly sighted in the 1960s by a wildlife biologist. Never confirmed, never fully debunked. The swamp has a way of playing tricks on the imagination
- **Freshwater mussels:** Look for mussel shells on muddy banks. Freshwater mussels are one of the most endangered groups of animals in North America — they filter massive quantities of water and are sensitive indicators of water quality. Their presence here is a good sign
- **Pearl River logging history:** The Pearl River basin was heavily logged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries — cypress and hardwoods were extracted for New Orleans construction. You can still find old logging canals cut into the swamp. The forest has recovered, but old-growth cypress is essentially gone from this area

### Honey Island Swamp Spiel — Ecology (5–10 min)

- **Cypress swamp function:** Bald cypress swamps are among the most productive ecosystems on earth. They filter water, store carbon, buffer storm surge, and provide habitat for hundreds of species. Less than 20% of Louisiana's original cypress swamps remain
- **Saltwater encroachment:** Freshwater swamps like this one are threatened by rising sea levels and human alterations to river flow. When salt water intrudes, cypress trees die — that's what you see in the "ghost forests" near the coast. It's one of the most visible signs of Louisiana's land loss crisis
- **Old New Orleans landscape:** Before development, New Orleans was ringed by cypress swamp, which gave way to brackish marsh, then salt marsh at the coast. Look at an overhead map and you can still see this gradient. The seafood Louisiana is famous for — shrimp, crabs, oysters — depends on those brackish and salt marshes existing and functioning
- **Gulf coast restoration:** Louisiana has a state coastal master plan that includes river diversions to reintroduce freshwater and sediment into the dying marshes. These are engineering projects on an enormous scale. The science supports it but the politics are complicated. End on the hopeful note: this swamp exists, it's functioning, and we're paddling through it

### Birds to Know (Manchac)
- **Bald Eagle:** Look for the white head and tail on a large, dark-bodied bird soaring overhead
- **Barred Owl / Great Horned Owl:** Listen for the barred owl's "who cooks for you" call; great horned owls are often roosting in cypress crowns
- **Osprey:** Hovers over water and dives feet-first — often confused with eagles but slimmer, with a white belly
- **Great Blue Heron:** The tall grey-blue stalker in the shallows; often seen hunting motionless
- **Anhinga:** The "snakebird" — swims with its body submerged, only neck visible; spreads wings to dry afterward
- **Prothonotary Warbler:** Brilliant golden-yellow; nests in tree cavities over the water; a swamp specialist
- **White Ibis:** Bright white with a curved orange bill; often in flocks
- **Limpkin:** Brown, spotted; makes one of the most unsettling calls in North America — a loud, strangled wail

## Part 7: Emergency Procedures

### If a Guest Capsizes
1. Call out calmly: "Stay with your boat. I'm coming."
2. Paddle to them immediately; keep other guests in place with a horizontal paddle signal (stop)
3. Help them re-enter the kayak: guide them to the back deck, have them kick and pull up from the side
4. If they cannot re-enter: tow kayak to nearest stable shore; assist them out of the water
5. Assess for injuries, cold, or shock
6. Continue tour if the guest is comfortable and willing; head back if there is any doubt

### If a Guest Has a Medical Emergency (heart issue, allergic reaction, seizure, etc.)
1. Call 911 immediately
2. Contact management while waiting for emergency services — have your GPS coordinates ready (or Plus Code)
3. Begin CPR if trained and indicated
4. Keep other guests calm and away from the situation
5. Do not move a guest who may have a spinal injury
6. Fill out an incident report as soon as the immediate emergency is resolved

### If Lightning is Spotted
1. Head to shore immediately — do not wait for thunder; if you see lightning, move
2. Get guests out of kayaks and away from the water
3. Seek shelter in the van if accessible; avoid tall isolated trees
4. Contact management; assess whether the tour can resume or should be called
5. Guests are not charged if the tour is cut short due to weather

### If You Have a Vehicle Incident (accident, breakdown)
1. Ensure guest safety first
2. Call 911 if there are injuries or the vehicle is blocking traffic
3. Contact management immediately
4. Do not leave guests unattended
5. Fill out an incident report

## Part 8: Code of Conduct

Violations are taken seriously. The standard is firm because guest safety and the business's reputation depend on consistent professionalism.

### Required at All Times
- Wear the NKST uniform shirt during every tour
- Maintain a professional, courteous tone with all guests and vendors
- Be on time. Arriving late to pickup is not acceptable

### Prohibited
- Smoking, vaping, or use of tobacco products around guests or in the van
- Alcohol or drug use before or during any tour — zero tolerance. Arriving hungover is treated the same as arriving impaired
- Personal phone use while driving the van (or trailer) — ever
- Profanity in front of guests
- Feeding, baiting, or harassing wildlife — this violates eco-tour principles and potentially the law
- Allowing guests on the water without life jackets
- Leaving any guest alone on the water
- Using the rope swing — do not use it, do not allow guests to use it. It is dangerous
- Personal use of company vehicles

### Tour Humor — What Works and What Doesn't

**A one-liner that works:**
> "Have your guests put their hand in the water. Tell them to smell it. After a few answers, tell them: 'I smell Adventure.'"

**What does not belong on a tour:**
- Stories about how drunk you were last night
- Asking guests what their favorite alcoholic drink is
- Swearing, sexual innuendos, or dirty jokes
- Anything you wouldn't say in front of a 10-year-old and their grandparent at the same time

The rule of thumb: your guests are on vacation. They came for the swamp, the wildlife, and a good story. Give them that.

### Violation Protocol
- **First violation:** Guide is assigned to clean and maintain equipment; re-reads this SOP in full; written acknowledgment to management
- **Second violation:** Management review meeting; probationary period; potential suspension
- **Third violation:** Contract termination
