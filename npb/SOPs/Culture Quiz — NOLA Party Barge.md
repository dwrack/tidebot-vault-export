# How We Run Things — The Quiz
### NOLA Party Barge

*For new hires. Read each scenario. Pick your gut answer. Then read the takeaway.*

---

### Scenario 1: The Extra Body

A bachelorette group booked for 12. They show up with 14. The bride is stressed because two friends "just got in last night." The boat's capacity is 16 so technically they fit. But they only paid for 12.

**What do you do?**

A) Let them on. It's a bachelorette. Don't kill the vibe.
B) "Absolutely, I can add them. It's $50 each, and I'll need them to sign waivers before we push off. Two minutes, we'll get it sorted."
C) Tell them no extras, 12 is 12

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Own Your Patch.** Option B. You just made the business $100, made the bride happy, AND kept it above board with waivers. Letting them on free devalues the product. Turning them away ruins the experience. Owning your patch means finding the move that's good for the guest AND good for the business.
</details>

---

### Scenario 2: The DJ Request

Your group wants to play their own playlist through the speakers. It's explicit, loud, and you're about to cruise past a family-oriented section of the waterway.

**What do you do?**

A) Let them play whatever. It's their party.
B) "Love the playlist. We're about to pass some families so let me turn it down for about 5 minutes, then we'll crank it back up once we're past."
C) Only play the approved playlist. No exceptions.

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real.** Option B. You're not a rule enforcer. You're also not going to blast explicit music past kids on a dock. Read the situation, be honest about why, and keep the energy up. Most groups respect it when you're straight with them. "Approved playlists only" is corporate. "Let me turn it down for a sec" is human.
</details>

---

### Scenario 3: The One More Drink

A guest is clearly hammered. They're stumbling, slurring, and trying to climb on the railing. Their friends are laughing about it. The trip has 45 minutes left.

**What do you do?**

A) Keep an eye on them but let the friends manage it
B) Talk to the most sober friend privately: "Hey, I need your help keeping [name] seated for the rest of the trip. If they go near that railing again, I'm pulling over."
C) Cut the trip short and dock immediately

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job.** Option B first, Option C if it doesn't work. You're the captain. You're responsible for everyone on this boat. The friends laughing doesn't make it safe. One fall off a railing is a drowning risk, a lawsuit, and the kind of situation that ends careers. Be direct, be calm, and be willing to dock if the situation doesn't improve. Nobody has ever been fired for being too careful.
</details>

---

### Scenario 4: The Broken Speaker

The Bluetooth speaker died mid-trip. No backup. The group is bummed. You've got 90 minutes left on the water.

**What do you do?**

A) Apologize and offer a partial refund
B) Turn it into the best unplanned moment of the trip. Announce a captain's log. Get everyone to share their best NOLA story. Start a sing-along. Make it weird and memorable.
C) Dock early and refund the time

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real + Own Your Patch.** Option B. The speaker dying sucks. But the captain who turns a problem into the best part of the trip is worth more than any sound system. This is the stuff people talk about in their 5-star review: "The speaker broke and then our captain turned it into the best part." Your personality IS the product. The speaker is just a prop.
</details>

---

### Scenario 5: The Bad Review Waiting to Happen

You overhear a couple at the end of the trip saying they were disappointed. "I thought there'd be more stops" and "It was fine, just not what we expected." They haven't left yet.

**What do you do?**

A) Let them go. Can't please everyone.
B) Walk over: "Hey, I overheard a bit of that. I want to make sure you had a great time. What were you hoping for? Let me see if there's something I can do."
C) Report it to management so they can follow up later

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Respect the Work.** Option B. A 3-star review is a 5-star review that nobody bothered to save. Two minutes of honest conversation at the dock can fix what a follow-up email can't. You might not solve it. But the effort itself changes how people feel about the experience. "The captain actually asked what he could have done better" is the kind of thing that turns a mediocre review into a good one.
</details>
