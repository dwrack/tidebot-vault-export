# NOLA Party Barge — Crew & Captain Performance Guide
*Updated 2026-03-30 | For all crew and captains*

> **This isn't just a checklist. It's how you become the reason people talk about this experience for years.**

---

## Why This Job Is Actually Awesome

You are not moving boats and cleaning restrooms. You are the host of one of the best moments in someone's trip — sometimes in their entire year. Bachelorette parties, birthdays, anniversaries, first trips to NOLA, last nights before someone moves away. You're there for all of it.

The people on your boat came to have the time of their lives. You are the person who makes that happen — or doesn't. That's real responsibility, and it's also real power.

The best crew aren't just doing tasks. They're reading the room, building energy, throwing in a story at the right moment, making the shy guest feel included, and walking off the boat at the end of the day with a pocket full of tips because every single person felt like the trip was made for them.

That's the job. This guide tells you exactly how to do it.

---

## The Big Picture: How Every Trip Should Feel

Think of every trip as an emotional arc — not just a checklist of stops.

```
ARRIVAL → BUILD → PEAK → LAND → SEND OFF
```

- **Arrival:** Guests are excited but uncertain. Your job is to make them feel welcomed and safe immediately.
- **Build:** Energy rises. Music, movement, NOLA stories, questions, laughter.
- **Peak:** The moment they'll talk about. The best song, the wildest story, the photo they'll post.
- **Land:** Energy eases. Guests are glowing, connected. Set up the tip and review ask.
- **Send Off:** They leave feeling like they just had something they couldn't have found anywhere else.

**You should know at every moment where you are in this arc.** A flat trip where nothing builds is forgettable. One great moment + a strong close = a 5-star review.

## Arrival Duties by Boat

*Before boat prep. Non-negotiable. Work is checked daily.*

### FREAKY TIKI
- Set up and clean the outdoor customer area
- Arrange chairs, tables, cushions, and shade sails
- Inspect customer restrooms — cleanliness, soap, toilet paper, paper towels
- Ensure everything is clean and properly placed

### TWERKIN TIKI
- Start at the roadside and work toward the property
- Sweep and pick up all trash and debris from both land and water
- Empty all trash cans and replace liners
- Remove any unsightly items and dispose of all waste in the dumpster

### BAYOU BOOGIE
- Sweep, clean, and scrub the docks to remove dust and debris
- Once finished, assist the Freaky Tiki deckhand with prepping the customer area

### PARTY QUEEN
- Inspect all potted plants and trees — water anything that looks dry
- Walk the full property — check that landscaping looks healthy and well-maintained
- Rake and even out any displaced landscaping materials (rocks, dirt, mulch)

> **If not all boats are going out:** All hands focus on the customer area and restrooms. That's what guests see first. First impressions are everything.

- [ ] Playlist ready and queued (see Music section below)

**Cleaning**

- [ ] Sweep floors or rake turf (tiki boats)
- [ ] Clean restroom — toilet, sink, floor
- [ ] Check toilet paper, soap, paper towels — restock if low

**Supplies**
- [ ] Paper towels, toilet paper, garbage bags, spray, water loaded
- [ ] Cooler drained and cleaned out
- [ ] All supplies staged and ready

**USCG Safety Check (Captain — Required Before Every Departure)**
- [ ] Engine inspection — oil, fuel, bilge, battery charge
- [ ] Navigation lights operational
- [ ] Life jackets counted — USCG Type I/II/III, sufficient for max capacity
- [ ] Throwable PFD (Type IV) on deck and accessible
- [ ] Fire extinguisher charged and accessible
- [ ] Visual distress signals (flares) present and in-date

> The USCG items are not optional and are not just paperwork. They are the legal and physical protection for every person on that boat. Any item flagged — report to the manager before guests board.

## Pre-Departure: The Guest Experience Starts Now

*This window is the most underused part of the trip. Use it.*

**Timeline**
- **-15 min:** Boat is ready. You are at your station. Music is playing. Vibes are set.
- **-5 min:** Help guests onboard with food and drinks. Be warm, make eye contact, use names if you have the manifest.
- **-2 min:** Standing at the boat entry to collect and count tickets.
- **0 min:** Ticket count matches FareHarbor manifest. Tickets dropped in the designated box. You are ready to go.

**The Welcome**

This is your first impression. Don't waste it on logistics. Open with energy.

*Instead of:* "Welcome aboard, please find a seat, here are the rules..."

*Try:* "Welcome to [Boat Name]! Y'all picked a perfect day for this. We're about to show you why NOLA is the greatest city in the world from the best seat in it. We've got [X minutes] together — let's make it count."

Then do a quick, confident safety briefing:

> *"Real quick before we head out — life jackets are under/at [location] and are required for anyone under 13. Stay seated while we're moving unless your deckhand says it's good. Throwable floatie is right here at [location]. Fire extinguisher is at [location]. If you hear the horn going continuous, follow crew instructions. That's it. Any questions? Good. Let's go."*

**The Opening Question**

Right after the briefing, before you pull out, throw one question to the group. It breaks the ice, gives you intel, and makes guests feel like this is a conversation, not a service transaction.

Great openers:
- *"What are we celebrating today?"*
- *"Who here has been on a boat in NOLA before?"*
- *"First time in New Orleans? What made you finally come?"*
- *"What's the wildest thing anyone's done this trip so far?"*

Then actually listen. Their answer tells you who they are and how to calibrate the energy for the next two hours.

## Storytelling: Your NOLA Material

*You don't need to be a historian. You need 3–5 great stories and the confidence to tell them. Here's your toolkit.*

### Why Stories Matter

A tour built around facts is forgettable. A tour built around stories is a conversation people have for years. The best guides don't deliver information — they take you somewhere.

The rule: **one surprising detail beats five correct facts.**

---

### The City Itself

**NOLA sits in a bowl — and it shouldn't exist**
New Orleans was built in 1718 on swampy ground below sea level. The French picked this spot not because it was smart, but because it was strategically positioned between the Gulf and the interior of the continent. The river bends in a crescent shape around the city — that's where "Crescent City" comes from. Every time you're on this water, you're floating in the same spot people have been floating in for 300 years.

**The river runs the city**
The Mississippi carries more water past this city than most people can comprehend — about 400 billion gallons a day. Everything that ever made NOLA great — the food, the culture, the music, the wealth, the chaos — all of it traces back to this river. The city was built to move goods. What it became was something else entirely.

**Laissez les bons temps rouler**
"Let the good times roll." That's not a slogan — it's a survival mechanism. This city has been hit by hurricanes, floods, yellow fever, fires, you name it. The response to all of it was the same: throw a parade, play some music, and keep going. That spirit is still here, and you're floating in it right now.

---

### Music

**Jazz was born here**
NOLA didn't just produce great musicians — it invented an entire art form. Congo Square, just a mile from the French Quarter, was one of the only places in the American South where enslaved people were allowed to gather and play music on Sundays. What came out of that — the rhythms, the call-and-response, the improvisation — became jazz, which became the foundation for basically all American popular music.

Louis Armstrong grew up poor in the Tremé, one of the oldest Black neighborhoods in the country. He taught himself to play in a home for troubled youth and became the most influential musician of the 20th century. He's buried here.

**The second line**
A second line parade is what happens when NOLA throws a party on the street. The "first line" is the brass band and the official group. The "second line" is everyone else — strangers who fall in and dance behind them. That's it. No permission needed. You hear the music, you join. That's very NOLA.

**Big Freedia and Bounce**
If jazz is NOLA's musical history, Bounce is its present. Big Freedia, born and raised here, turned this city's street music into a global phenomenon. Bounce is loud, joyful, and completely its own thing. When it plays on this boat, you're not listening to generic party music — you're listening to something that came from the streets of this exact city.

### Local Color (Use These Casually)

These are things locals know that visitors don't. Drop them naturally — they make guests feel like insiders.

- **Neutral ground** — what everyone else calls a median. In NOLA it's the neutral ground, dating back to when the French and Americans occupied opposite sides of Canal Street and that strip in the middle was the literal neutral territory between them.
- **Making groceries** — going to the grocery store. Not "getting" groceries. Making them. This is just how NOLA talks.
- **Lagniappe** (lan-YAP) — a little something extra. Bakers used to throw in a 13th item when you bought a dozen. Now it means any unexpected bonus. If you do something especially cool for a guest, that's lagniappe.
- **Dressed** — if you order a po'boy "dressed," that means lettuce, tomato, pickles, and mayo. If you just say you want a po'boy, that's not enough information.
- **Who Dat** — Saints fans know. If you see someone wearing black and gold, "Who Dat" is the greeting.
- **The Garden District** — the neighborhood where the old American money built mansions to prove they were just as good as the French Creoles in the Quarter. If the French Quarter is old New Orleans, the Garden District is old American New Orleans. Anne Rice lived there. Brad Pitt and Angelina Jolie had a place there.

---

### NOLA on the Water

**The lake and the bayou**
Lake Pontchartrain to the north is technically a brackish lake (part saltwater, part fresh) — it connects to the Gulf through Lake Borgne. The bayous are the slow-moving waterways that thread through the wetlands. Louisiana loses a football field of coastal land to the Gulf every 100 minutes because of coastal erosion and subsidence. The wetlands that protect the city are disappearing faster than anyone wants to think about.

**The levees**
The levee system is what keeps the city from flooding — mostly. Katrina showed what happens when it fails. The system has been massively upgraded since 2005. But the physics haven't changed: this city is below sea level, surrounded by water, in hurricane country. The fact that it's here, and that it's still itself, is kind of remarkable.

## Guest Questions: The Real Skill

*Most crew deliver information. The best crew have conversations. This is the difference.*

The rule from the best guides in the world: **almost no one asks guests a real question beyond "where are you from?"** If you do this, you're already in the top 5%.

### What Makes a Good Question

- Open-ended — can't be answered with yes or no
- Anyone can answer it without special knowledge
- Sparks a follow-up — doesn't dead-end

### Questions to Use (In Order of the Trip)

**At boarding — intelligence gathering**
- *"What are we celebrating today?"*
- *"First time in NOLA, or are you coming back for more?"*
- *"What's been the best part of the trip so far?"*

**Mid-trip — connection and depth**
- *"What's the most surprising thing about this city so far?"*
- *"Does this remind you of anywhere you've been?"*
- *"What made y'all pick a boat trip?"*

**Toward the end — set up the close**
- *"What was your favorite moment out there?"*
- *"What are you taking home from today?"*
- *"What would you tell someone to do if they asked about this trip?"*

That last question is gold. When they answer it, they're literally practicing the story they're about to tell their friends. And that story is your word-of-mouth.

### Rules for Questions

1. **Ask and then shut up.** Silence after a question is not awkward — it means the guest is thinking. Resist the urge to fill it.
2. **Don't ask and then answer yourself.** Worse than not asking at all.
3. **Rotate.** Don't just talk to the loudest person. The quiet guest who didn't self-select into the conversation is the one who will write the review later.
4. **Use their names.** If you caught names at boarding, use them when you direct a question. People light up.

## Music: Building the Vibe

*Music is not background noise. It's the emotional backbone of the trip. Use it intentionally.*

### The Arc

Match your playlist to the emotional arc of the trip:

| Phase | Vibe | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Boarding** | Warm, energetic, welcoming | NOLA classics, upbeat soul, funky |
| **Departure** | Building energy, fun, gets people moving | Trombone Shorty, Galactic, The Meters |
| **Peak** | Full party mode, danceable, crowd pleasers | Big Freedia, current hits, Bounce |
| **Landing** | Easing back, still feel-good | Slower soul, classic NOLA |
| **Send off** | Warm closer | Something iconic, singable |

### Your NOLA Playlist Foundation

**Classic NOLA DNA**
- Dr. John — *Right Place Wrong Time*, *Such a Night*
- The Meters — *Cissy Strut*, *Hey Pocky A-Way*
- Fats Domino — *Blueberry Hill*, *Walking to New Orleans*
- Louis Armstrong — *What a Wonderful World*, *When the Saints Go Marching In*
- Allen Toussaint — *Yes We Can Can*, *Southern Nights*

**Brass Band / Second Line Energy**
- Rebirth Brass Band — *Do Whatcha Wanna*, *Feel Like Funkin' It Up*
- Dirty Dozen Brass Band
- Trombone Shorty — *Backatown*, *Hurricane Season*
- Galactic — *Gris-Gris Strut*, *Kayfabe*

**Modern NOLA**
- Big Freedia — *Explode*, *Gin in My System*
- Juvenile — *Back That Azz Up*, *Slow Motion*
- Lil Wayne — anything in the catalog is deeply NOLA
- Bounce genre playlist

**Universal Party Starters (crowd pleasers for non-NOLA guests)**
- Lizzo, Beyoncé, Bruno Mars — read the crowd
- If it's a bachelorette party, they probably have a playlist. Play it.

### Reading the Room

- If guests are standing and moving — turn it up, let it ride
- If guests are sitting and talking — match the energy, don't fight it
- If the vibe is dropping — a well-timed song flip can save the trip
- Silence is almost never the right call
- **Ask the group:** *"Anyone have a song request?"* — this one move makes guests feel ownership of the experience

### Key Rule

**Never let a moment of transition go silent.** The space between songs, while you're docking, while guests are boarding — that's when the vibe dies. Fill it. Even a transition can be a moment.

## Designing the Wow Moment

*Every trip needs one moment that breaks the expected rhythm. Plan yours.*

This is the thing guests talk about. Not the whole trip — one specific moment. If you can design it, you guarantee the story they tell.

For a party barge, the wow is usually:
- The moment the music really hits and everyone starts dancing at once
- A story or fact that genuinely surprises the group
- Something you do personally that they didn't expect
- A view, a moment, a coincidence you make more out of than they expected

### How to Engineer It

1. **Build to it.** Don't just let it happen — narrate toward it. *"In about 10 minutes we're going to be at the best spot on this water. I want you to look at where we are right now and compare."*
2. **Let it breathe.** When the moment hits, don't immediately talk over it. Let the music play, let the view land, let the group feel it together.
3. **Name it after.** *"That's what I'm talking about. You can't get that anywhere else."*

The best wow moments are simple. A perfect song at a perfect moment. A story no one saw coming. You noticing that one guest is having a rough day and doing something small to change it.

## Tips: How to Earn What You're Worth

*Better service doesn't automatically mean better tips. The architecture of your close does.*

### The Truth About Tips

The research is clear: people tip based on emotional state at the moment of the ask, not a precise calculation of service quality. If the last moment of the trip is flat, the tip reflects that — even if the trip was great. If the last moment is warm, funny, and personal, the tip reflects that.

Your job is to make sure the last 5 minutes of the trip are the best 5 minutes.

### Prime Early (Don't Wait Until the End)

- When you do the safety briefing, mention: *"And just so you know — our crew runs on tips. If we take care of you today, we'd love it if you took care of us at the end."* — casual, early, normalizes it.
- During the trip: if a guest asks about the crew, that's your moment to mention it naturally.

### The Close (Last 5–10 Minutes)

**Step 1: Highlight reel**
Walk them back through the trip verbally. *"From when we first pulled out and hit that music, to [specific moment], to right now — y'all made this an easy day."* This reconstructs the value in their mind.

**Step 2: Reflection question**
*"Before you head out — what was the favorite part for you?"* Let people answer. Collective positive emotion = generosity mode.

**Step 3: The ask**
Keep it personal and human, not scripted:

> *"Y'all — I'm not going to lie, this is how I pay my rent, and days like today remind me why I love this job. If we took care of you, our crew would love it if you took care of us. Venmo/Cash App/[method] — whatever's easiest. And if you've got 60 seconds, a Google review goes a long way. We're out here every day — your review is how the next group finds us."*

**Step 4: Make it easy**
QR code for tips and reviews. Hold it out while people are still in that warm, post-trip glow. Don't let them walk away and do it later. They won't.

### The Group Photo

Take a group photo at the best moment of the trip. Offer to send it to them. This is a reciprocity trigger — when you give them something, they want to give back.

*"Give me your number and I'll text you the photo — no spam, just the photo."*

Then text it within the hour. Follow up with: *"Great having you today — if you get a chance, a Google review means the world to us: [link]"*

## Site Check: No Equipment in Customer Areas

*This is non-negotiable. Guests should never see tools, gas cans, extension cords, power tools, vacuums, or any work equipment in customer areas. Ever.*

**Why this matters:** When guests arrive and see circle saws on tables, gas cans in the gazebos, and extension cords strung across walkways, they don't see a marina that's improving. They see a construction site. They also see tripping hazards and safety problems. It kills the vibe before the trip even starts, and it makes us look like we don't have our act together.

**The rule:** Customer areas must be completely clear of all equipment, tools, and hazards **30 minutes before the first check-in of the day.**

**Site Check rotation:** One crew member per day is assigned as Site Check owner.

- **Morning (before first check-in):** Walk all customer areas. Confirm everything is clear. Take a photo and post to #marina-daily in Slack.
- **End of day (after last tour):** Same walk, same photo, same channel.
- If something is left out by someone else, photo it, post it to the Slack channel, and tag the person responsible.
- Three flags in a month = conversation with Jeffrey.

**Where equipment goes:** All tools, cords, gas cans, and supplies go back to the equipment storage area. If you use it, you put it back. If you see something out, you put it back and flag who left it.

## What Not to Call Jeffrey About

The goal is for you to handle everything on this list without escalating. Here's the framework:

**Handle it yourself:**
- Guest questions about NOLA, the boat, the trip
- Minor complaints (cold beer, music volume, seating)
- Normal checklist items
- Tips disputes between crew (follow the 70/30 pooled split policy, see `Tip Split Policy.md`)
- Weather that's clearly fine

**Use your judgment, then text if needed:**
- Mechanical issue that seems minor but you're not sure
- Guest who's overly intoxicated — follow the policy, then inform
- Headcount discrepancy on FareHarbor

**Call immediately:**
- Medical emergency
- USCG safety item that can't be resolved before departure
- Guest injury on the boat
- Significant mechanical failure
- Anything that puts the departure in question

> Most things that crew call managers about are things crew can handle. If you're unsure, ask yourself: "Do I actually need Jeffrey to handle this, or do I just need someone to know about it?" If it's the latter, send a text.

## The Crew Culture Standard

**3 strikes policy is real.** Jeffrey checks the work. But the better frame is: this operation runs on reputation. A clean boat, a great crew, a guest who tells 10 friends — that's what keeps you employed and well-tipped.

The standard isn't "complete the checklist." The standard is: **"Would I be proud for Jeffrey to watch me do this job right now?"**

If yes, keep going.
If no, adjust.

That's it.
