# The First 15 Minutes

*For all NOLA Party Barge captains. This is not a suggestion. This is the standard.*

---

## The Captain's Station

**10 minutes before departure, you are at your post.**

Your station is at the entrance to your boat. Not on the boat. Not in the back. Right where passengers step on. Music is already playing. You are the first face they see and the first hand they shake.

Crew handles final cleaning and prep. You handle the people.

This is the moment that sets the tone for the entire trip. If you're distracted, checking your phone, or hiding in the wheelhouse, they feel it. If you're standing tall, smiling, greeting them by name when you can, they feel that too.

**What you're doing at the station:**
- Greeting every guest as they approach
- Offering a hand to help them board (especially the heels-and-bodysuit crowd, which is most of your guests)
- Asking the group what they're celebrating
- Pointing them to where to set down coolers and bags
- Reading the energy of the group so you know how to calibrate the trip

---

## The Welcome (60 Seconds)

Once everyone is aboard and settled, this is your opening. Say it in your own voice, but hit every beat.

> *"Welcome aboard [Boat Name]. I'm [Captain Name], I'll be your captain today.*
>
> *Before we get into it, I need to cover the important stuff real quick.*
>
> *Life jackets are at [location], required for anyone under 13. If you need one, ask. The throwable floatation device is right here at [location]. Fire extinguishers are here and here [point]. First aid kit is at [location]. If you hear the horn going continuous, that means listen up and follow crew instructions.*
>
> *While we're moving, stay seated unless your deckhand says otherwise. And please, keep hands and feet inside the boat. This water's got things in it that don't want to meet you.*
>
> *That's the serious part. Now here's the fun part.*
>
> *You're sitting on the best seat in New Orleans right now. This river, this bayou, this water, it built this whole city. Everything you love about NOLA started right here. And for the next [X hours], it's all yours.*
>
> *So what are we celebrating today?"*

Then shut up and listen. Their answer tells you everything you need to know about the next two hours.

**The beats you must hit (USCG required):**
1. Your name and role as captain
2. Life jacket locations and donning (required under 13, available for all)
3. Throwable PFD location
4. Fire extinguisher locations
5. First aid kit location
6. Emergency signal (continuous horn = follow crew instructions)
7. Stay seated while moving
8. Person overboard awareness (keep hands/feet inside)

Miss any of these and you're out of compliance. The Coast Guard doesn't care if your vibe was great.

## The Pre-Tour Captain's Log

**This is mandatory. USCG requires a trip log for every departure.**

Before you leave the dock, record your pre-tour log. Audio recording on your phone is fine. Upload it to the **#captains-log** Slack channel before departure.

**What the log covers:**

> *"[Date], [Time], [Boat Name], Captain [Name], Deckhand [Name].*
> *[Number] passengers aboard, manifest confirmed.*
> *Safety equipment check complete: [PFDs counted], fire extinguishers charged, throwable PFD on deck, flares in date, horn functional, nav lights operational, radio on Ch 16, first aid stocked.*
> *Engine check: oil good, fuel good, bilge clear, battery charged.*
> *COI and license on board.*
> *Weather: [conditions]. Water: [conditions].*
> *Departing [time]."*

This takes 45 seconds. It protects you, it protects the company, and it proves we run a tight operation if the Coast Guard ever steps on board.

---

## The Post-Tour Captain's Log

**After docking and guests have departed.** Same channel, same format.

> *"[Date], [Time], [Boat Name], Captain [Name].*
> *[Number] passengers, trip complete.*
> *Any incidents: [none / describe].*
> *Any mechanical issues: [none / describe].*
> *Any guest concerns: [none / describe].*
> *Boat secured, lines tied, motors trimmed, batteries off."*

30 seconds. Do it while the trip is still fresh. Don't wait until you get home.

---

## Why This Matters

The welcome and the logs aren't separate things. They're the same discipline.

The captain who nails the welcome is the captain who runs a safe boat. The captain who records the log is the captain who catches the mechanical issue before it becomes a breakdown. The captain who greets guests at the station is the captain who gets the big tips and the five-star reviews.

It's all connected. Professionalism at the start creates trust, trust creates fun, fun creates tips, tips create income. Skip the start and you're leaving money on the deck.

## Mid-Week Coaching (Wednesdays)

Every Wednesday, JT or Jeffrey posts a short message in the crew Slack. This isn't a lecture. It's a stoke.

**Template:**

> *Big weekend coming up. [Number] tours booked.*
>
> *Coaching note from last weekend: [specific positive example from a captain or deckhand, e.g., "Eddie's welcome on Saturday was dialed in, his group tipped $200. Go listen to his pre-tour recording."]*
>
> *One thing to work on: [specific, actionable, e.g., "Make sure the safety briefing doesn't sound rushed. Slow down, make eye contact, own it."]*
>
> *Reminder: pre-tour and post-tour logs uploaded to #captains-log every trip. No exceptions.*
>
> *Let's get it this weekend.*

Keep it under 100 words. Lead with what's working, then one thing to improve. Captains and crew respond to recognition, not criticism.
