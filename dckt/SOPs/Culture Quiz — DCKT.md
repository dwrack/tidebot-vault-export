# How We Run Things — The Quiz
### Door County Kayak Tours

*For new hires. Read each scenario. Pick your gut answer. Then read the takeaway. No wrong answers, just honest ones.*

*Managers: walk through these together on orientation day. The conversation matters more than the "score."*

---

### Scenario 1: The Empty Slot

It's a Tuesday in June. You check the schedule and see a 2pm tour with only 2 people booked. Your buddy texts asking if you want to grab lunch. The weather is perfect.

**What do you do?**

A) Text your manager asking if the tour will cancel
B) Show up ready to give those 2 people the best tour of their summer
C) Suggest combining it with the 10am tour so you can have the afternoon off

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Own Your Patch.** Those 2 guests might have driven 3 hours to be here. They picked your tour. They might tip better than a group of 12, write the review that books your next 50 tours, or become regulars who come back every year. The tour is the tour, whether it's 2 people or 14. If you're only excited when the van is full, this isn't the right fit.
</details>

### Scenario 2: The Instagram Guide

A guest on your tour is barely paying attention to the wildlife because they're filming everything for Instagram. They keep asking you to "say that again but louder" and want you to pose with an animal for their Story. The rest of the group is getting annoyed.

**What do you do?**

A) Play along. Content is good for business.
B) Ignore them and focus on the rest of the group
C) Find a middle ground. "Hey, I love that you're capturing this. How about I give you a killer photo spot in about 10 minutes, and right now let's all take in this moment together?"

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real.** You're not a content creator's prop. You're also not a jerk about it. The move is being genuine: acknowledge what they want, redirect with something better, and protect the experience for everyone. A guide who performs for one person's phone at the expense of the group has lost the plot. A guide who shuts it down harshly isn't being real either. Find the human middle.
</details>

---

### Scenario 3: The Gear Shortcut

You're loading up for a morning tour. You notice one of the life jackets has a buckle that's getting hard to click. It still works if you push it hard enough. You've got 10 minutes before guests arrive and the spare jackets are locked in the back storage unit.

**What do you do?**

A) Use it. It still works, just takes extra effort.
B) Grab the spare, even if it makes you 5 minutes late starting
C) Use it for yourself and give the good ones to guests

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job.** Grab the spare. Period. A buckle that "still works if you push it" will fail the one time it matters. Being 5 minutes late to start is nothing compared to a guest struggling with a life jacket in the water. And using the sketchy one yourself isn't noble, it's still a safety problem. Flag the broken one for repair before it goes back in rotation.
</details>

### Scenario 4: The Quiet Problem

You overhear another guide complaining to guests about the schedule, saying things like "they always overbook us" and "management doesn't really get what it's like out here." The guests look uncomfortable. You know this guide has been frustrated but hasn't brought it up with anyone.

**What do you do?**

A) Stay out of it. Not your business.
B) Talk to the guide privately after the tour. "Hey, I heard some of that. You should bring it up with [manager]. Venting to guests isn't going to fix it."
C) Report it to management immediately

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Respect the Work.** Option B. Direct, private, peer-to-peer. You're not tattling, you're looking out for them. Complaining to guests doesn't fix anything and it makes everyone look bad. The guide has a real frustration, and it deserves a real conversation with someone who can actually change things. If they won't bring it up, you can offer to back them up when they do. That's respect.
</details>

---

### Scenario 5: The Better Way

You've been doing tours for a few weeks. You realized that if you adjust the launch order (putting beginners in first, experienced paddlers last), the group stays tighter and the tour flows way better. Your manager hasn't mentioned this approach.

**What do you do?**

A) Just do it your way. It works better.
B) Keep doing it the old way. The SOP says what it says.
C) Share it. Drop a message in the channel: "Tried something different with launch order today, here's what happened." Let the team decide if it's worth adopting.

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Get It Out of Your Head.** Option C. You found something better. Now share it so everyone benefits. The SOPs aren't sacred texts carved in stone. They're living documents built by the people doing the work. But "just doing it your way" without telling anyone means it disappears when you're not on the schedule. And blindly following a process you know is worse isn't loyalty, it's laziness. Share what you learned. That's how we all get better.
</details>
