# How We Run Things — The Quiz
### Lone Star Kayak Tours

*For new hires. Read each scenario. Pick your gut answer. Then read the takeaway.*

---

### Scenario 1: The Nervous Group

A family of 5 shows up. Mom is visibly nervous. Dad is pretending he's not. The kids are excited but the youngest (maybe 10) is asking if there are alligators. This is clearly their first time kayaking.

**What do you do?**

A) Give the standard safety briefing and get them on the water
B) Spend an extra 5 minutes with them at the launch. Get the youngest to help you hold a paddle. Show Mom how stable the kayak is by rocking it yourself. Make it feel easy before anyone gets in.
C) Suggest they do a shorter route

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Own Your Patch.** Option B. Five extra minutes turns a nervous family into your best review of the week. The 10-year-old who helped you hold the paddle is going to talk about this for months. Mom relaxing means Dad relaxes means everyone has fun. You're not just running a tour, you're reading the group and giving them what they need before they know they need it.
</details>

---

### Scenario 2: The "I've Done This Before" Guy

A guest tells you he's an experienced kayaker and doesn't need the safety briefing. He's already getting in the kayak while you're still talking to the group.

**What do you do?**

A) Let him go. He knows what he's doing.
B) "Awesome, love having experienced paddlers out here. I still need everyone to hear the full briefing, it's a Coast Guard requirement. Plus there are a couple things specific to this waterway that'll make your trip better. Two minutes, I promise."
C) Tell him he has to wait or he can't go

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job.** Option B. You don't skip the briefing for anyone. But you don't have to be a hard-ass about it either. Frame it as information he'd want, not a rule he has to follow. "Coast Guard requirement" is true and shuts down the argument. Experienced paddlers are often the ones who get complacent, and this waterway has its own hazards.
</details>

### Scenario 3: The Competitor Question

A guest asks "Have you done [competitor's name]'s tours? Are they any good?" They're clearly comparing options for a friend visiting next month.

**What do you do?**

A) Trash the competitor
B) "I haven't personally, but I know they run a different kind of trip. Ours is more [what makes you different]. If your friend wants [what you offer], send them our way."
C) Say "I don't know anything about them"

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real.** Option B. You don't need to trash anyone. You also don't need to pretend you don't know the market. Be honest about what makes your tour different and let the guest decide. Confidence in what you do beats insecurity about what someone else does. And lying ("I don't know anything about them") is transparent and weird.
</details>

---

### Scenario 4: The Post-Tour Mess

You get back from a tour. The next guide's trip leaves in 45 minutes. The kayaks need hosing, the van needs trash cleared, and one of the paddles has a small crack you noticed mid-tour. You're tired and your shift is technically over.

**What do you do?**

A) Leave it. The next guide can handle it.
B) Hose the kayaks, clear the van, flag the cracked paddle in the channel with a photo, and let the next guide know in person if they're there.
C) Just flag the paddle and leave the rest

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Respect the Work.** Option B. The person after you is counting on clean gear and a ready van. Leaving a mess isn't just lazy, it's disrespectful to your teammate. And the cracked paddle is a safety flag that needs documentation, not just a mental note. Photo in the channel, verbal heads-up, done. That's 10 minutes of your time that saves someone else a bad day.
</details>

### Scenario 5: The Wildlife Moment

Mid-tour, you spot a bird you've never seen before. A guest who seems to know birds gets excited and starts explaining what it is. You learn something.

**What do you do?**

A) Cool moment, move on
B) Thank the guest, then after the tour drop it in the channel: "Guest spotted a [bird] near [location]. Apparently they're rare here. Adding it to my tour knowledge."
C) Pretend you already knew what it was

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Get It Out of Your Head.** Option B. You just learned something from a guest. That's not embarrassing, that's great. And now every guide on the team knows about it. Next week, another guide can say "We've had a [bird] spotted near [location] recently" and sound like a wildlife expert. The knowledge compounds. But only if you share it.
</details>
