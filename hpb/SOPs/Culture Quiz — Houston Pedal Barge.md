# How We Run Things — The Quiz
### Houston Pedal Barge

*For new hires. Read each scenario. Pick your gut answer. Then read the takeaway.*

### Scenario 1: The Late Group

Your 4pm trip is scheduled. It's 4:10 and only half the group is here. The ones who showed up on time are getting antsy. The booking contact texts: "5 more minutes, they're parking."

**What do you do?**

A) Wait. They paid for the trip.
B) Start your pre-departure routine with the guests who are here. Safety briefing, music setup, drinks settled. When the rest arrive, catch them up in 60 seconds. You haven't lost any time.
C) Leave at 4:15 sharp regardless

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Own Your Patch.** Option B. You're not a bus driver on a fixed schedule. You're also not going to punish the people who showed up on time. Use the wait productively. Get the energy started, get waivers signed, get the vibe going. When the late ones arrive, they walk into a party that's already rolling. That's better than everyone standing around awkwardly.
</details>

### Scenario 2: The Photo Request

A group asks you to take their photo from the dock before departure. Then one more. Then a group shot. Then individuals. It's been 10 minutes of photos and you haven't left the dock.

**What do you do?**

A) Keep taking photos until they're done
B) "I'll grab one more great one, then how about I get some action shots once we're out on the water? The lighting out there is way better anyway."
C) Tell them we need to leave now

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real.** Option B. You're helpful, you're not a photographer. Redirect with something better instead of shutting it down. And you're not lying: the on-the-water photos actually are better. You've managed their expectation, moved the trip forward, and given them something to look forward to. That's reading the room.
</details>

### Scenario 3: The Capacity Call

You're doing the head count before departure. You count 17 bodies. Capacity is 16. The booking says 16. Someone brought an extra friend "who's tiny, she won't take up space."

**What do you do?**

A) She's tiny, one more won't matter
B) "I totally get it. Coast Guard capacity is 16 and I can't go over that, it's not my call. But I can see if there's availability on the next trip and we'll get her on board."
C) Tell the whole group someone has to stay behind

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job.** Option B. Capacity limits exist because of weight, stability, and life jacket counts, not because someone decided to be difficult. "It's not my call" is your friend here. Blame the Coast Guard, blame the regulations, but don't bend. And offering an alternative shows you're trying to help, not just saying no. The one time you let number 17 on is the one time something goes wrong.
</details>

### Scenario 4: The Repeat Customer

A group comes back for their third trip. They love you. They ask to book directly with you next time instead of going through the website. "Can we just text you?"

**What do you do?**

A) Give them your number. Loyalty matters.
B) "That means a lot. The website is actually the best way because it locks in your spot and handles the waivers automatically. But drop a review and mention my name, that's the best thing you can do for me."
C) Ignore the request

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Respect the Work + Get It Out of Your Head.** Option B. You're flattered, and you should be. But side-channel bookings create chaos: no waivers, no payment tracking, scheduling conflicts. The system exists to protect everyone. Redirect them into the system AND capture the value (review with your name). Then post in the channel: "Third-time group today, asked to book direct with me. Sent them to the site." That tells management you've got repeat customers, which is worth knowing.
</details>

### Scenario 5: The Engine Sound

Mid-trip, you notice a slight grinding sound from the motor that wasn't there before. It's subtle. Everything is still working fine. You've got an hour left on the water.

**What do you do?**

A) Keep going. It's probably nothing.
B) Note the sound mentally and check it after you dock
C) Let the group know you need 2 minutes to check something. Inspect visually, listen again. If it's unclear, dock and check properly. Post in the channel immediately with a voice memo of the sound if you can capture it.

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job + Get It Out of Your Head.** Option C. "Probably nothing" is how things become something on the water. You don't have to panic, but you do have to check. And capturing the sound for the next captain and the maintenance crew means you're not relying on "it was making a weird noise" from memory. Specific, documented, shared. That's how you prevent problems instead of reacting to them.
</details>
