### Scenario 1: The Early Arrivals

Your 6pm session doesn't start for 20 minutes. A couple pulls into the parking lot early, gets out of their car, and starts walking toward the dock looking a little lost. You're in the middle of setting up.

**What do you do?**

A) Wave and let them know you'll be ready soon
B) Walk over, greet them by name (check the booking), show them around, offer water, and make them feel like they just arrived at a friend's place
C) Finish setup first, then greet them at the scheduled time

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Own Your Patch + Be Real.** Option B. The experience starts the second they park. "It's great to see you" hits different than "We'll be ready in 20." You don't have to stop setup, just make them feel seen. First impressions are 90% of reviews. A nervous couple who gets greeted warmly before they even reach the dock is going to relax into the experience instead of wondering if they're in the right place.
</details>

### Scenario 2: The Cold Plunge Pusher

A group of 4 is here for the first time. Three of them are stoked for the cold plunge. One is clearly anxious about it but feeling peer pressure to do it. Their friends keep saying "Come on, you HAVE to try it."

**What do you do?**

A) Encourage them to try it. That's the experience.
B) Pull the anxious person aside: "Hey, there's zero pressure. Some people love it, some people just do the sauna and that's perfect. You can always change your mind later."
C) Tell the whole group to stop pressuring their friend

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Be Real.** Option B. Privately, gently, no judgment. The anxious person needs permission NOT to do it. If they end up trying it on their own terms, that's a better story than being peer-pressured into a panic. If they don't try it, they still had a great time. A host who protects someone's comfort is more memorable than a host who pushes the product.
</details>

### Scenario 3: The Repeat Member

A member you recognize shows up for their session, but the booking is under someone else's name. When you check, it looks like they might be using another member's guest pass. The member acts like everything is normal.

**What do you do?**

A) Let it go. They're a regular. Don't make it weird.
B) "Hey [name], great to see you. Quick thing: this booking is under [other name]'s pass. Let me pull up your account and make sure everything's right so you don't have any issues."
C) Refuse entry until it's sorted

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Respect the Work.** Option B. Friendly, not accusatory, but you don't look the other way. Membership abuse devalues the product for everyone who pays fairly. Handle it warmly but handle it. If it's a mistake, no harm done. If it's a pattern, you've flagged it. Ignoring it because someone is "nice" is exactly how standards erode.
</details>

### Scenario 4: The Temperature Complaint

Mid-session, a guest comes out of the sauna and says "It doesn't feel hot enough." The thermometer reads 175F, which is normal. They seem disappointed.

**What do you do?**

A) Tell them the temp is normal and the thermometer confirms it
B) "Good feedback. Sometimes humidity affects how the heat feels. Try pouring a little water on the rocks, sit on the upper bench, and give it a few minutes. If it still doesn't feel right, come find me and I'll check the system."
C) Crank the heat

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Get It Out of Your Head.** Option B handles the guest, but here's the real move: after the session, drop it in the channel. "Guest said it didn't feel hot enough at 175F. Told them to try the upper bench and löyly. Worked, they loved it." Now every host knows how to handle that question. AND if it keeps coming up, maybe we do need to look at the system. One person's complaint is data. Five people's complaints are a pattern. You only see the pattern if everyone shares.
</details>

### Scenario 5: The Slippery Dock

You notice the dock walkway is a little slick from morning dew. It's not terrible, but you can feel it under your feet. Guests are arriving in 15 minutes.

**What do you do?**

A) Keep an eye on it and warn guests verbally
B) Grab a towel, dry the worst spots, put down the dock mat if we have one, and mention it to guests as they walk down. Then post it in the channel: "Dock was slick this morning from dew. Dried it and put the mat down. Might need to add this to the pre-session checklist."
C) It'll dry on its own by the time people arrive

<details>
<summary>The takeaway</summary>

**Safety Is the Job.** Option B. A slip on a wet dock is a broken wrist, a lawsuit, and a closed Saturday. You don't wait for it to dry. You don't just warn people. You fix it AND you make sure it gets added to the process so the next host doesn't have to figure it out on their own. Safety + systems = the whole point.
</details>
