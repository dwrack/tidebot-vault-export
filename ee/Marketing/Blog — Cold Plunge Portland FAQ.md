# Portland's Only Snowmelt Cold Plunge (It's Not in a Tub)

Most "cold plunge" experiences in Portland involve a stainless steel tub in a dimly lit room, chilled to a carefully controlled 50°F, with ambient music and a timer on the wall.

This isn't that.

At Ebb and Ember, your cold plunge is the Columbia River: actual Cascade snowmelt, 40+ feet deep, flowing from the shoulders of Mt. Hood straight past our floating sauna in North Portland. You jump off the boat. That's it. No filter, no thermostat, no timer telling you when to get out.

Here's what people ask us most.

---

## How cold is the Columbia River right now?

As of spring 2026, the Columbia is running colder than usual, and that's saying something.

Oregon's snowpack hit record-low water content this year as of April 1, the lowest ever recorded for that date. That matters because the Columbia is literally fed by that snowmelt. Less snowpack means the melt comes faster and earlier, which shifts the temperature curve in ways we're still watching play out.

In winter, the river sits around 42°F. To put that in perspective: 42°F is the temperature of a glass of ice water that's been sitting on your counter for about 10 minutes. Now picture that glass is 40 feet deep, moving, and stretching a mile wide in front of you.

In summer, the river warms into the mid-to-upper 60s, which sounds mild until you've been sitting in a 180°F sauna for 20 minutes and your body doesn't know which way is up anymore.

We keep an eye on NOAA river data so we can give guests a heads-up on current temps before their session. But honestly, the number matters less than you think. What matters is the contrast: from the heat of the sauna to the shock of the river. That contrast is the whole point.

## Can you actually cold plunge in the Columbia River?

Yes. People have been getting into the Columbia for as long as people have lived here. What's changed is access.

Portland's relationship with its rivers has been complicated. For decades, the waterfront was industrial, fenced off, or just not somewhere you'd want to swim. Organizations like the Human Access Project have been pushing to change that, advocating for the public's right to safely access the rivers that run through this city. That work matters, and it's part of why experiences like ours exist.

At Ebb and Ember, you're not bushwhacking down a muddy bank hoping for the best. You're stepping onto a purpose-built floating sauna with two ladders into the water, two throw rings, life jackets available, and other people around. You can jump off the lower deck or, if you're feeling it, off the roof. The river at our dock is over 40 feet deep depending on the season, so there's plenty of clearance.

This isn't some rogue swim spot. It's a real, intentional way to access the river.

## Is the Columbia River safe to jump into?

We're going to be honest here rather than just say "yes, come on in."

The Columbia is a big river. It's powerful, it's cold, and it deserves respect. We take safety seriously: life jackets on board, throw rings within reach, two ladders so you can always get out, and you're never in the water alone. Buddy system, always.

But safety isn't just equipment. It's mindset. We tell every guest the same thing: **when in doubt, don't go out.** If something feels off, if the water looks different than expected, if your body is telling you to wait, listen to that. The river will be there next time.

Cold water is where you find the boundary of your comfort zone. That's the whole value of it. Growth lives right at that edge. But the edge is personal, and pushing past it recklessly isn't brave, it's just reckless. Coming back and going a little further next time? That's the move.

Start on the ladder. Get waist deep. Breathe. See how your body responds. You don't have to jump off the roof your first time. Or ever.

## What does "snowmelt cold plunge" mean?

We use this term because it describes exactly what you're getting into, and because it's nothing like what most people picture when they hear "cold plunge."

A snowmelt cold plunge means the water you're jumping into started as snow on the Cascades. It melted off Mt. Hood, Bull Run, the high ridges east of Portland, flowed into tributaries, and eventually merged into the Columbia River, which passes right under our sauna boat.

It's not recirculated. It's not treated. It's not sitting in a basin waiting for you. It's alive, it's moving, and it's carrying minerals and oxygen from hundreds of miles of mountain terrain.

The experience is fundamentally different from a cold plunge tub. Tubs are still water in a controlled box. The river has current. It has depth. It has temperature variation depending on the season, the snowpack, and what the mountains are doing that year. In 2026, with record-low snowpack across the entire West, the melt pattern is running earlier and faster than normal, which means the cold hit is shifting too.

Every time you get in, the river is slightly different. That's not a bug. That's the whole thing.

## How is a river cold plunge different from an ice bath?

Bigger than you think.

An ice bath is still water in a container. Your body heats the water directly around your skin within seconds, creating a thin warm layer. That's why people stir ice baths, to break that layer and keep the cold consistent. It's effective, but it's engineered discomfort.

A river doesn't let you build that warm layer. The current pulls cold water across your skin constantly. There's no microclimate to hide in. The cold is moving, and so is your response to it.

Then there's the mental side. Stepping into a tub you can see the bottom of is one thing. Jumping into 40+ feet of dark, flowing river water is a completely different conversation with your nervous system. Your brain processes open water differently than a controlled environment. It's more activating, more primal, more honest.

And the contrast cycle matters. Coming out of a 180°F sauna on a floating boat and dropping into 42°F snowmelt is a swing you can't replicate in a studio. The heat is real heat. The cold is real cold. Your body doesn't have to guess.

## Can you cold plunge year round in Portland?

Yes, and the experience changes with the seasons.

**Winter (Dec-Feb):** River temps sit around 42°F. This is the deep end of cold plunging. Short immersions, huge adrenaline response, the kind of cold that makes you laugh involuntarily when you surface. The sauna feels like a miracle after.

**Spring (Mar-May):** Temps start climbing from the mid-40s into the 50s as snowmelt increases. This year, with record-low snowpack, the warming curve may shift earlier than usual. Spring is arguably the best time, cold enough to feel it, warm enough to stay in a bit longer.

**Summer (Jun-Aug):** Upper 50s to mid-60s. The contrast with the sauna is still significant, and you can actually hang in the water and enjoy it. This is when people start jumping off the roof without hesitating.

**Fall (Sep-Nov):** The river cools back down through the 50s into the 40s. Fewer people on the water, quieter sessions, beautiful light on the river.

We run year round. The river doesn't close.
